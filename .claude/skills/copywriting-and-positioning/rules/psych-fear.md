## Use fear to make the threat real, then offer the specific solution

**Impact: HIGH** — Fear is one of the most studied motivators in consumer research. Constructed correctly it drives action; constructed wrongly it triggers denial and the prospect tunes out.

Fear-based copy works **only when all four conditions are met**:

1. **The threat is vivid and personally relevant** — it scares *this* prospect, not "people in general."
2. **A specific recommendation is given** — what to do, not just what to fear.
3. **The recommendation is perceived as effective** — proof it actually neutralizes the threat.
4. **The prospect believes they can perform the action** — it's affordable, easy, and within reach.

Miss any one and fear backfires: condition 1 → reader feels nothing; condition 2 → paralysis; condition 3 → cynicism; condition 4 → learned helplessness. Generic fear ("germs are bad") loses to specific, sensory, named fear ("10% of a two-year-old pillow's weight is dead mites and their feces").

**Don't write this — vague threat, abstract solution, no path to action:**

```text
Headline: Stay safe from germs in your home.

Body: There are many germs in your home that can make your family
sick. Our disinfectant spray helps fight germs and keeps surfaces
clean. Buy it today and protect your loved ones.
```

Why it fails: no vivid threat (no scene, no numbers, no named diseases), no proof the solution works, no easy action. Conditions 1 and 3 fail. Reader feels nothing and does nothing.

**Write this instead — vivid threat, named solution, quantified efficacy, trivial action:**

```text
Headline: Your kitchen floor is a cesspool of bacteria — and your
crawling baby is putting it in her mouth right now.

Body: One single bacteria cell explodes into 8 million cells in
under 24 hours. The invisible microbes on the surfaces your child
touches every day cause strep throat, pneumonia, meningitis, skin
disease, urinary tract infections, and the flu.

The fix: Lysol® Disinfectant Spray kills 99.9% of germs on
commonly-touched surfaces. About $5 a can, available at any
grocery store. Pick one up on your next trip.
```

Why it works:
- **Threat is vivid (cond. 1)** — specific scene (baby on floor), specific stat (8M cells in 24h), specific named diseases.
- **Solution is named (cond. 2)** — Lysol Disinfectant Spray, not "a disinfectant."
- **Efficacy is quantified (cond. 3)** — 99.9% kill rate.
- **Action is trivial (cond. 4)** — $5, any grocery store, next trip already planned.

### Common mistakes

- **Fear without solution** → paralysis or denial. Always pair the threat with the fix in the same eye-span.
- **Generic "scary stuff"** without sensory specifics → reads as background noise the reader has heard before.
- **Solution requires effort the prospect doesn't believe they can do** → kills condition 4. ("Apply 17 times a day with surgical precision.")
- **Wrong LF8 trigger for the product.** A luxury watch sells on want #6 (be superior), not fear. Match the appeal to what the product actually delivers.
- **Cartoon-level fear** that insults the reader's intelligence → cynicism. Stay in the realm of believable, evidenced threats.

### When to apply

- Product solves a survival, safety, health, or financial-loss problem.
- Prospect is unaware of the threat (highest leverage) or aware but underestimating it.
- You can satisfy all four fear-appeal conditions above.
- You have specific, sensory detail available — named pathogens, real numbers, vivid scenes.

### When NOT to apply

- Aspirational/status products — use ego (`psych-ego.md`).
- Audience that would feel manipulated or condescended to (sophisticated B2B buyers, premium luxury).
- You can't deliver condition 3 or 4 (no real efficacy proof, or solution is expensive/hard).
- The product is genuinely about pleasure, beauty, or social approval — fear is the wrong lever.
