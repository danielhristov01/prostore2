## To shift a buyer's belief, change its focus or its importance — don't try to overturn it

**Impact: MEDIUM** — People defend their beliefs as if survival depends on them, even beliefs they suspect are wrong. Direct contradiction ("you're wrong about X") triggers active resistance. Two indirect routes work better: change what the belief is *about* (focus), or change how *important* it is (importance).

**Route 1: Change the focus.** Introduce a new fact that points the existing belief at a different target. The belief stays intact; its target moves. Example: prospect believes "cheap brands are fine for kitchen tools." You don't argue. You introduce: "the kitchen tool that touches your food at 400°F is worth paying for — leaching at high heat is a real thing." The belief survives ("cheap is fine for low-stakes tools") but no longer applies to YOUR category.

**Route 2: Change the importance.** Strengthen or weaken an existing belief without changing it. Easier than overturning. Reinforce wanted beliefs with stats, examples, testimonials; gently demote unwanted beliefs by introducing competing priorities.

**Don't write this — head-on contradiction of an existing belief:**

```text
Headline: Stop believing baking soda whitens teeth. It doesn't.

Body: Despite popular opinion, baking soda toothpaste has no
proven therapeutic value. Switch to our advanced whitening
formula instead.
```

Why it fails: tells the prospect they were wrong. They'll dig in. The "stop believing" framing triggers defense, not openness. Even if the data backs you up, the buyer's identity ("I'm someone who knows about home remedies") is now under attack and they'll resist.

**Write this instead — change the focus / shift importance:**

```text
Headline: The American Dental Association on baking soda:
"feels good, but no proven therapeutic value."

Body: Baking soda IS great for one thing — that fresh, clean
mouthfeel after brushing. So we kept it. We just added the
hydrogen peroxide complex that actually whitens (the
ingredient pro whitening services use), and combined them in
one toothpaste.

Same fresh feel you already love. Plus the whitening that
baking soda alone can't deliver. Best of both.
```

Why it works:
- **Belief survives** — "baking soda is good" stays; the prospect doesn't have to admit being wrong.
- **Focus shifts** — baking soda is now framed as good *for mouthfeel*, not for whitening.
- **Importance shifts** — the whitening claim moves to the new ingredient, demoting baking soda's whitening importance without attacking it.
- **Authority transfer** (`psych-transfer-credibility.md`) — the ADA quote is doing the heavy lift, not us.

### Common mistakes

- **Frontal contradiction** — "you're wrong" is the fastest way to entrench. Always indirect.
- **Trying to change the belief itself** instead of focus/importance. Belief change is rare and expensive; reframing is cheap and reliable.
- **Weakening a belief the prospect uses for self-image** — risks triggering identity defense. Reframe gently or pick a different belief.
- **Using authority crudely** — citing a study to "prove the prospect wrong" is just frontal contradiction with extra steps.

### When to apply

- Categories where a competing folk-belief or competitor narrative is in the way.
- Premium products competing against "good enough" beliefs.
- Repositioning campaigns where the goal is to change category perception.

### When NOT to apply

- When the belief is true and harmless. Don't fight beliefs you don't need to fight.
- When you can simply work *with* the belief instead of around it (faster).
- Identity-loaded beliefs (political, religious, deep cultural) — high risk, low reward in commercial copy.
