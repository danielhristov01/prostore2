## Organize the message — clear structure outranks clever content

**Impact: HIGH** — *"Understand if you saying don't what I'm, persuade I can't you."*

If the prospect can't follow your message, no other technique in this skill can rescue it. Even with great products, slick visuals, and glowing testimonials, an ad that's structurally disorganized leaves the reader with an inaccurate takeaway, an incomplete picture, or no engagement at all. Worse, a confusing ad can damage your brand for a long time — the reader's only takeaway is "I didn't get it" or "they don't have it together."

**Organization first. Then content. Then style.**

### The AIDA backbone

The default direct-response structure (`body-granny-rule.md`):

1. **Attention** — headline grabs (`headline-biggest-benefit.md`).
2. **Interest** — opening builds rapport, names the problem.
3. **Desire** — benefits, mental movies, specificity.
4. **Action** — explicit, easy, scarce CTA.

Every persuasive piece should be checkable against this sequence: does each section do its job? Does each section bridge to the next?

### The five-stage progression (longer pieces)

For long copy, the Cashvertising checklist:

1. Get attention.
2. Stimulate interest.
3. Build desire.
4. Offer proof.
5. Ask for action.

Same idea, with **Proof** broken out as its own stage. For high-stakes purchases, proof needs its own dedicated section, not just inline mentions.

### Hierarchy and signposting

Within each section:

- **Subheads break long copy** — let the skim-reader pick up the thread anywhere.
- **Bullet/numbered lists** — for benefits, features, steps. Easier to scan than prose.
- **Bolded emphasis** — to mark the most important phrases. Sparingly, or it loses signal.
- **One thought per sentence**, **one idea per paragraph** — see `structure-simplicity.md`.
- **Captions under images** — heavily read because the eye stops at images. Use that real estate to sell.

**Don't write this — disorganized stream-of-consciousness:**

```text
[All in one big block]

Buy our coffee maker. It's amazing. We've been around 25 years.
The motor has 1500 watts. Free shipping. Customers love us!
Warranty included. 12 settings. Coffee tastes great. Available
in 3 colors. Did we mention free shipping? Order now or call
1-800-XXX-XXXX. Founded by two brothers in 1999.
```

Why it fails: no AIDA. Attention, interest, desire, proof, action all jumbled and stepping on each other. Reader can't follow. Even the Free Shipping mention loses force from being mentioned twice in the chaos.

**Write this instead — organized through AIDA:**

```text
[Attention — Headline]
The coffee maker that brews your morning cup in 47 seconds —
without the bitter aftertaste of fast brewers.

[Interest — Opening]
If you've owned a "fast" coffee maker before, you know the
trade-off: speed for taste. Until now.

[Desire — Benefits]
Three things make the X1 different:
• 1500W heating element brings water to 195°F in 38 seconds
• Patented gentle-flow nozzle prevents the over-extraction
  that causes bitterness
• 12 brewing settings let you tune the strength

[Mental movie of using it]
You hit the button. By the time you've put on your shoes,
your cup is ready.

[Proof]
4.7 stars from 8,400 reviews on Amazon. Recommended by Cook's
Illustrated, March 2024. 3-year warranty.

[Action — CTA]
Order today and get free shipping. Your X1 ships within 24
hours.

[Order Now button]

The 47-second cup is waiting.
```

Why it works:
- AIDA sequence is intact and visible.
- Each section has one job and bridges to the next.
- Skimmer can read just headers + bullets + CTA and still get the pitch.
- Engaged reader gets the full case.

### Common mistakes

- **No identifiable structure** — paragraphs in random order.
- **Burying the lead** — biggest benefit (`headline-biggest-benefit.md`) appears in the body instead of the headline.
- **Missing AIDA sections** — copy that has Attention but skips to Action with no Interest/Desire/Proof.
- **Multiple competing structures** — copy trying to be a sales letter, a product page, AND a brochure simultaneously.
- **No bridges between sections** — sections that just stop and start, instead of leading the reader from one to the next.
- **Long copy without subheads** — readers can't navigate; they bounce.

### When to apply

- Every persuasive piece. Structure is upstream of every other rule.
- When auditing copy that "isn't working" — check structure before content.
- Long-form pages especially.

### When NOT to apply

- Very short formats (single-line ads, tweet-length copy) where the AIDA sequence collapses to one or two beats — but even there, attention-then-action is structure.
