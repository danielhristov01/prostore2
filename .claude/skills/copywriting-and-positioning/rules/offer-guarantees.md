## Offer a long, strong, specific guarantee — and make it longer than your competitors'

**Impact: HIGH** — Buyers feel vulnerable. Between paying and using, there's a stress window — "what if this doesn't work? what if it's not what I thought?" The higher the price, the heavier the stress. A strong guarantee absorbs the stress and removes the biggest pre-purchase fear: loss.

Counterintuitive but well-documented: **longer guarantees boost sales AND lower return rates.**

- Short guarantee (30/60/90 days): customer stays poised to return, watches the calendar, mentally prepared to act before the deadline.
- Long guarantee (6 months / 1 year / lifetime): customer relaxes, integrates the product into their life, never returns it.

The "beat the clock" mindset is what drives short-guarantee returns. Long guarantees defuse it.

Bonus effect: a long guarantee lets you publicly ask, "Why do our competitors guarantee their [product] for only 90 days?" — followed by, "Do they know something about their [product] they're not telling you?" Inoculation (`psych-inoculation.md`) and competitive pressure in one move.

**Don't write this — short, weak, hedged guarantee:**

```text
Body: 30-day money-back guarantee. Restocking fees may apply.
Original packaging required. Some exclusions apply.
```

Why it fails: short window, hedged with friction. Reads as "we'll fight you on returns." Stress is *not* removed; it's amplified.

**Write this instead — long, specific, friction-free guarantee:**

```text
Body: 365-day Triple Guarantee:

1. If it doesn't [do specific outcome] within 60 days, ship it
   back for a full refund. No questions, no restocking, no
   "original packaging required." Just send it back.

2. If anything breaks within a year, we replace it. Even if you
   ran it over with your truck. (Yes, that happens. Yes, we
   replaced it.)

3. If at any point in the first year you find the [product] for
   less elsewhere, we refund the difference plus 10%.

Why so generous? Because we know what most companies don't want
you to know: most "30-day guarantees" exist to make you watch
the calendar. We'd rather you forget about the guarantee and
just enjoy the product.
```

Why it works:
- **365 days** absorbs the "what if it doesn't work" stress.
- **Three layers** show the company has thought through every fear.
- **Specific examples** (truck, price drop) make the guarantee feel real, not legalese.
- **Anti-competitor framing** at the end primes the prospect against shorter guarantees they'll see elsewhere.
- Lower return rate is the empirical bonus.

### Guarantee structure ingredients

- **Time** — longer than competitors. 1 year > 90 days. Lifetime > 1 year.
- **No-friction return** — no restocking fee, no original packaging requirement, no return-shipping cost.
- **Specific outcome** — guarantee against a defined failure mode ("If it doesn't reduce your processing time by 30%…"). Stronger than generic "satisfaction guaranteed."
- **Replacement / repair / refund options** — multiple paths, not single-rail.
- **Personal voice** — "I personally guarantee" beats "the company guarantees."
- **Reasoning** — explain *why* the guarantee is so generous. Reasoning increases believability.

### Common mistakes

- **Short guarantee paired with friction** — guarantees that read like obstacle courses scare buyers worse than no guarantee at all.
- **Hidden guarantee** — buried in a footer, in the FAQ, in the fine print. The guarantee should be in the hero, in the cart, in the email — anywhere fear lives.
- **Vague guarantee** — "satisfaction guaranteed" without saying *how* the dissatisfaction gets resolved.
- **Guarantee without scarcity** — pair them. Guarantee removes risk; scarcity removes deferral. Both at once is the strongest CTA setup.
- **Not using the guarantee competitively** — if your competitors offer 30 days and you offer 365, say so. Loudly.

### When to apply

- Almost always for direct-response.
- Highest leverage on high-ticket purchases (the stress window is biggest).
- Subscription/SaaS — money-back guarantees on first month/year.
- Any product where buyer fear of being-stuck-with-it is a real obstacle.

### When NOT to apply

- Categories where return is impractical (perishables, custom goods, software you can copy and keep). Adapt: longer support window, free re-do, free consultation, etc.
- Premium luxury where guarantees can read as desperate. Replace with concierge service language.
- When you can't afford to honor it. A guarantee you'd default on is fraud.
