## Use color — and prefer blue/red/green when choosing — to lift attention and engagement

**Impact: MEDIUM** — Color isn't decoration; it's an attention and engagement lift with measurable returns. Starch Research:

- Color ads encourage **~60% more in-depth reading** than black-and-white.
- Color ads encourage **~40% more in-depth reading** than two-color (limited palette) ads.
- The color → engagement gradient is real and measurable.

When you do choose colors, consumer preference rankings (averaged across studies):

### Single-color preference

| Rank | Color |
|---|---|
| 1 | Blue |
| 2 | Red |
| 3 | Green |
| 4 | Violet |
| 5 | Orange |
| 6 | Yellow |

### Color-pair preference (interesting because consumer preference ≠ complementary theory)

| Pairing | Complementary rank | Consumer preference rank |
|---|---|---|
| Blue–Yellow | 2 | **1 (most preferred)** |
| Blue–Red | 10 (least complementary) | **2** |
| Purple–Yellow | 5 | 3 |
| Red–Green | 1 (most complementary) | 4 |
| Violet–Green | 3 | 5 |
| Red–Green (variant) | 7 | 6 |
| Blue–Orange | 8 | 7 |
| Red–Orange | 9 | 8 |
| Green–Yellow | 4 | 9 |
| Purple–Orange | 6 | 10 |

Notable mismatch: **Red–Green is most complementary in color theory but ranks only 4th in consumer preference**, while **Blue–Red is the *least* complementary but ranks 2nd in preference**. Don't assume color theory predicts preference.

### Practical takeaways

- **Use color** — pretty much always, unless you have a deliberate B&W positioning.
- **When picking a brand pair, Blue+Yellow leads** — possibly why so many high-recognition brands (IKEA, Walmart, Visa, etc.) use this pairing.
- **Blue dominates single-color preference** — partly why so many trustworthy/financial/B2B brands lean blue.
- **Test in your specific category** — preference rankings are population averages; your audience can deviate.

### Don't pick colors based purely on this list

Two reasons:

1. **Category conventions matter.** Coca-Cola owns red in soft drinks. When Berni Corp. designers changed Canada Dry's sugar-free ginger ale can from red-and-white to green-and-white, sales jumped over 25% — because the red can confused buyers into thinking "cola" (`layout-color.md`).
2. **Brand differentiation matters.** If every competitor in your category is blue, going blue gets you lost in the pile. The right color is the one that's both *appealing* AND *differentiating* in your context.

Use this list as a tiebreaker, not a decree.

### Common mistakes

- **Skipping color entirely** to "look minimal" — usually a worse decision than people think. Add at least one strong accent color.
- **Stuffing too many colors** — 2-3 colors plus neutrals is a strong working palette. 5+ usually visual noise.
- **Picking from this list without checking category conventions** — see Canada Dry above.
- **Ignoring contrast/accessibility** — preference rankings don't account for legibility. Body copy must hold contrast regardless of palette.

### When to apply

- Brand identity decisions.
- Ad creative when color is a free variable.
- Refreshing legacy materials that are stuck in dated palettes.

### When NOT to apply

- When category convention strongly dictates a color and breaking convention would confuse buyers.
- When brand systems already exist — defer to the system.
