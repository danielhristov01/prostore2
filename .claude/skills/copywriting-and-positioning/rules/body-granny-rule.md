## Write the way a salesperson would talk to the prospect — meet them in their world

**Impact: HIGH** — Advertising is a salesperson in print, broadcasted to many. The best salespeople build rapport before they pitch — they meet the prospect in the prospect's world, find common ground, and earn the right to be heard. Copy that ignores this and opens with formal corporate distance ("Dear Customer, we are pleased to inform…") loses to copy that reads like an actual person speaking to an actual person they understand.

The Granny Rule (named for treating each prospect like a beloved relative you're writing to one-on-one) has a few practical implications:

1. **Personal voice** — write to one reader, not "everyone."
2. **Address them where they are** — if you're writing to teenagers, sound like one of their world ("Hey Justin… if you're sick of staying home every weekend doing nothing but surfing the web while all your friends are out partying…"). If you're writing to enterprise CTOs, sound like a peer engineer.
3. **Open with a question** — installs the open loop (`headline-power-of-questions.md`).
4. **Each sentence has one job: make them read the next sentence.** That's the chain.
5. **Tough talk: people open mail over a trash can. You have seconds.** Same goes for email subject lines, ads in feeds, web pages on first scroll.

### AIDA — the structural backbone

The classic direct-response sequence the Granny voice flows through:

- **A**ttention — the headline grabs.
- **I**nterest — the opening builds rapport and stakes.
- **D**esire — benefits and mental movies (`body-mental-movies.md`).
- **A**ction — the close.

Each section bridges to the next. No paragraph exists for its own sake.

**Don't write this — formal, distant, generic salutation:**

```text
Dear Customer,

We are pleased to inform you of our latest offering. Our company
has been a leader in the industry for over 25 years, and we
pride ourselves on customer satisfaction.

Please find enclosed information regarding our services. Should
you have any inquiries, do not hesitate to contact us.

Sincerely,
The Team
```

Why it fails: corporate distance, no rapport, no audience-specific voice, no question, no LF8 lever. Could be from any vendor. Goes in trash unread.

**Write this instead — specific, personal, in-their-world voice:**

```text
Hey Maya,

Quick question: what's the longest you've spent staring at your
production logs trying to figure out why a deploy broke at 2am?

For me it was 5 hours last March. By 7am I'd consumed 4 cups of
coffee, broken into a fresh tear of Slack pings from teammates
asking if it was fixed yet, and the actual root cause was a
typo in an env var I'd reviewed and approved myself the
afternoon before.

That morning is what made me build [tool]. It's not glamorous —
it just shows you the diff between your last working deploy and
the broken one in plain English. No 47 dashboards. No sifting.

If you've had a 5am morning like mine, the rest of this email
is for you.
```

Why it works:
- **Names the recipient** — first-person, one-to-one.
- **Opens with a question** — open loop installed.
- **Meets her in her world** — production logs, deploys, Slack, env vars. This voice would sound wrong to a marketing director, perfect to a senior engineer.
- **Vulnerable, specific story** — earns the right to pitch (rapport).
- **Bridges to action** — "the rest of this email is for you" segments aggressively, dignifying the reader's time.

### Common mistakes

- **"Dear Occupant"** — the universal mark of unread mail.
- **Talking about yourself before earning the right** — the first paragraph isn't about you; it's about meeting them. "We are pleased to announce…" is the tell.
- **Trying to write to everyone** — you write to one specific person. Even mass mail. The "one-to-one" voice scales; the "to-everyone" voice converts no one.
- **Failing to research the audience's world** — borrowed jargon used wrong reads worse than plain language. Either know the world or write plainly.
- **Dropping voice mid-piece** — opening warmly then shifting to corporate-speak in the body breaks the rapport.

### Important first-paragraph mechanics

- **Start the letter with a question** — extremely effective for getting people to read deeper.
- **The first sentence's only job: make them read the second sentence.**
- **The first paragraph's job: make them read the second paragraph.**
- And so on. Each unit of copy passes the baton, never tries to close on its own.

### When to apply

- Email — every email.
- Sales letters and long-form sales pages.
- Cold ad copy when targeting a specific niche.
- Personal/founder-led brands.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure transactional confirmation emails (order shipped, password reset) — the Granny voice would feel out of place.
- Highly regulated categories where compliance language is mandated.
- Some B2B contexts where executive-summary tone works better than narrative — but even there, "in their world" still applies; the world is just different.
