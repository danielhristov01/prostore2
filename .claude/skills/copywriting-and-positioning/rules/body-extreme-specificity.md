## Be extremely specific — exact numbers, named items, counted lists

**Impact: CRITICAL** — One of the highest-leverage edits in copywriting. Replace every general claim with a specific one and conversions move. The mechanism stacks two effects:

1. **Specificity reads as evidence.** "We have lots of fasteners" is a brag. "We carry 343 kinds of fasteners" reads like someone who actually counts. The brain treats specifics as having been *measured*, which is a credibility shortcut.
2. **Length-Implies-Strength heuristic** (`psych-heuristics.md`) — long, specific lists signal completeness. The reader's lazy brain doesn't audit; it absorbs volume as a quality signal.

The Cashvertising hardware-store example. Same business, two ads:

**Ad A — "Hammers, screwdrivers, power tools, home fix-up, lawn and garden equipment. Paulson's has the hardware you're looking for at neighborhood-friendly prices!"**

**Ad B — "Handyman Jack's is no ordinary hardware store. We carry 343 kinds of fasteners, 28 types of nails, 86 gauges of wire, 43 grit sizes of sandpaper, 16 different styles of hammers, 28 kinds of screwdrivers, 47 types of keys, a daily inventory of 354,000 bolts and screws, all the top-name power tools for less, and a no-nonsense, money-back guarantee of complete satisfaction."**

Same store, possibly identical inventory. Ad B reads as the bigger, more competent business. The numbers are doing all the work.

**The reader doesn't need any of those numbers.** They will never need 86 gauges of wire. The numbers function as a *signal of completeness*, not as functional inventory information. That's the asymmetry: the cost of being specific is one minute of word choice; the upside is permanently better perception.

**Don't write this — generalities everywhere:**

```text
Italian restaurant ad:

"We use only the best ingredients and prepare our food with
care. Our chefs have years of experience. Authentic Italian
flavors. Affordable prices."
```

Why it fails: every word is generic. Could describe any of 200,000 Italian restaurants in America. Reader's brain registers nothing.

**Write this instead — specifics replacing every generality:**

```text
Italian restaurant ad:

"We make our pasta fresh every morning at 6:15am. Our bread is
baked in a 700°F wood-fired oven, golden and crusty in 90
seconds. Our sauce simmers for 4 hours from San Marzano tomatoes
shipped weekly from Naples. We use only fresh herbs — basil and
oregano grown on-premises in our 200 sq ft rooftop garden. Pure,
cold-pressed 100% virgin olive oil. Our head chef Giacomo
trained for 11 years at Trattoria Sostanza in Florence before
opening here in 2014."
```

Why it works:
- Every claim has a number (6:15am, 700°F, 90 seconds, 4 hours, 200 sq ft, 11 years, 2014).
- Named items and places (San Marzano, Naples, Trattoria Sostanza, Florence).
- Process detail that no competitor would copy unless they did the same things.
- Reads as research, not marketing. The brain credibility-stamps it.

### How to extract specifics from generic copy

Edit pass: take any sentence and ask "what's the *specific* version of this?"

- "Years of experience" → 11 years
- "Many customers" → 4,200 customers
- "Fast" → in 28 minutes
- "Affordable" → $19.97
- "Fresh ingredients" → milled at 6:15am, picked yesterday morning
- "High quality" → SOC 2 Type II certified, 4.7/5 across 8,427 reviews
- "Powerful" → 4HP motor, 200 lb capacity
- "Saves time" → saves 23 minutes per workflow

Every "lots," "many," "great," "fast," "many years" is a specificity opportunity.

### Common mistakes

- **Round-number bias** — "thousands of customers" is round and feels marketed. "8,427 customers" feels measured. Odd numbers read more credible than round ones.
- **Specifics that aren't true** — fabrication eventually surfaces. Specifics must be real.
- **Specifics nobody cares about** — "Our office is 2,847 sq ft" is specific but irrelevant. The specifics must connect to a benefit or evidence claim.
- **Statistics-first when an example would lead better** (`proof-examples-vs-statistics.md`).
- **Burying specifics deep in the copy** — leading numbers/names live in headlines and openers, not paragraph 5.

### When to apply

- Every place general language currently appears. Audit by reading and circling every adjective.
- Headlines especially — replace any superlative with a specific number.
- Testimonials — push for specific outcomes when collecting.
- Hero copy.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure mood/lifestyle copy where specifics would feel clinical (high-end fragrance, some fashion). Even there, lean specific *somewhere* in the page.
- Brand voice that explicitly trades on mystery (rare positioning).
