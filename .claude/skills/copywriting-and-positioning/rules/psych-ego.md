## Use ego-morphing — make the product part of the prospect's identity

**Impact: HIGH** — When the product becomes part of the prospect's self-image, price resistance collapses. They aren't buying a thing; they're buying who they become when they own it.

The mechanism: pair the product with a carefully chosen image, lifestyle, or personality that the target prospect either already identifies with OR aspires to. The prospect transfers the image's attributes onto the product, then onto themselves through ownership. Done well, the product feels like an extension of identity rather than a transaction.

This works because most people have a gap between who they are and who they want to be. They will pay to close that gap. Your job is to position the product as the bridge.

**Don't write this — selling features instead of identity:**

```text
Headline: Premium Italian leather watch strap — $180.

Body: Made from full-grain Italian leather. Hand-stitched. Pairs
with all standard 22mm watches. Available in black, brown, and
tan.
```

Why it fails: features only. No image, no aspiration, no identity transfer. The reader has no reason to prefer this strap over any other premium leather strap.

**Write this instead — sell who they become:**

```text
Headline: For the man whose handshake is the deal.

Body: This isn't a strap you buy. It's a strap you've earned.
Hand-stitched in Florence by the same workshop that supplies
three of the four most respected watchmakers in Switzerland —
the kind of detail other men notice across a boardroom table.
Wear it the day you close.
```

Why it works:
- **Identity is named** — "the man whose handshake is the deal" is a self-image the buyer wants to claim.
- **Earned, not bought** — reframes the transaction as a milestone, not a purchase.
- **Aspirational signal** — Florence, Swiss watchmakers, boardroom — the product carries social signals the buyer absorbs by ownership.

### Common mistakes

- **Generic aspiration** — "successful people" is too vague to identify with. Name the *specific* person ("the man whose handshake is the deal," "the woman who runs the meeting before the meeting").
- **Mismatch between buyer and image** — projecting an image the buyer doesn't aspire to backfires. Research the audience first.
- **Over-promising transformation** — "buy this and you'll be successful" reads as snake oil. The product is the *signal* of identity, not the cause of it.
- **Using ego when the product can't deliver the signal** — if peers won't recognize the product, there's no identity transfer. Ego works for visible/social goods.

### When to apply

- Visible-to-peers products: cars, clothing, watches, homes, restaurants, business tools others see.
- Aspirational categories: luxury, status goods, premium services, professional tools.
- B2B "professional identity" purchases — the IDE, framework, or vendor someone tells colleagues they use.

### When NOT to apply

- Hidden/utility products no one else sees (in that case use `psych-fear.md` or LF8 wants 1, 3, 7).
- Audiences cynical to status appeals (engineers buying tooling — they want capability, not identity, though identity layers can still help).
- When the identity claim isn't credible — selling Rolls-Royce identity for a Hyundai breaks the spell.
