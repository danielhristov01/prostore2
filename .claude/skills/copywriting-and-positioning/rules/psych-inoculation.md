## Inoculate loyal customers with weak attacks so they defend you against competitors

**Impact: MEDIUM** — When you trigger a customer to actively defend their preference, the defending strengthens it. Like a vaccine: the weak attack stimulates antibodies. The more vigorously the customer mounts a defense, the more loyal they become.

The three-step pattern:

1. **Warn of an impending attack** — alert that competitor X is going to make claim Y.
2. **Make a weak attack** — present a flimsy version of the competitor's argument.
3. **Encourage a strong defense** — get the customer to verbalize or commit to their counter-argument.

The defense must be the customer's. If you fight the competitor for them, you fail to inoculate. The point is to get *them* to think systematically, find the holes in the weak attack themselves, and reinforce the position they already hold.

**CRITICAL caveat: the attack must be weak.** A strong counter-attack risks actually changing their attitude. You're vaccinating, not infecting.

**Don't write this — defending the brand FOR the customer (no inoculation, no defense built):**

```text
Email to existing customers:

"Acme Treadmills is launching next month with a 'cheaper' model.
Don't be fooled — we're still the best. Our motor is bigger, our
warranty is longer, and our customers love us more. Stick with us."
```

Why it fails: customer reads it passively. No defense generated. If anything, plants the idea that maybe Acme is a real option.

**Write this instead — flag, weak-attack, prompt their defense:**

```text
Email to existing customers:

Subject: You'll see Acme's new ads next month. Here's what's
coming.

Body: Acme is launching a $1,200 treadmill in 30 days. Their
ad will say it has the same 4HP motor as ours.

Honestly? On paper, that's not a crazy claim. The motor spec is
similar. So before the ads hit your feed, I want to ask you a
question — one you've already lived the answer to:

When you've used your treadmill three days a week for a year,
what would you tell someone shopping today that you couldn't
have known when you bought it?

(Hit reply. I read every one.)
```

Why it works:
- **Warning** — attack is flagged in advance (gives customer time to prepare defense).
- **Weak attack** — the conceded "on paper, motor is similar" is honest *and* weak. Doesn't trigger overcompensation.
- **Prompts defense** — the question forces the customer to articulate why they actually picked you (belt feel, customer service, build quality, whatever it is). They generate the defense in their own words.
- **Captures the defense** — the reply gives you testimonial gold AND deepens the customer's commitment.

### Common mistakes

- **Strong attack** — "Acme is launching a piece of junk!" risks the customer mentally testing the claim, finding it overstated, and concluding you're insecure. Weak attack only.
- **Doing the defending FOR them** — listing your own advantages is not inoculation; it's reassurance. Different mechanism, weaker effect.
- **Inoculating the wrong audience** — only works on existing preferers. Don't try to inoculate prospects who haven't picked you yet.
- **Attacking competitors who aren't on the customer's radar** — gives them oxygen. Only inoculate against threats the customer will encounter anyway.

### When to apply

- Existing-customer retention — especially before a known competitor launch.
- B2B account expansion where competitor displacement is active.
- Loyalty program emails, customer newsletters.
- Categories where the customer made an initial decision and a competitor is about to challenge it.

### When NOT to apply

- Prospect/acquisition copy — inoculation requires an existing preference to defend.
- When the competitor's claim is strong enough that a weak version still lands.
- Quiet markets — amplifying a competitor's argument when the customer wouldn't have heard it otherwise is a self-inflicted wound.
