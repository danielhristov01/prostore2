## Write to the chimpanzee brain — short words, short sentences, short paragraphs, plenty of "you"

**Impact: CRITICAL** — *"Write to the chimpanzee brain. Simply. Directly."* — Eugene Schwartz. *"Your ad gets only a fraction of their intelligence. People won't study your ad carefully. They can't be bothered. So you have to make your ads simple."* — John Caples.

Reading is voluntary. Every word the reader doesn't immediately understand, every sentence that requires a second pass, every paragraph that looks like work — costs you readers. Simplicity isn't dumbing down; it's removing friction between your message and their understanding.

Cashvertising's two-paragraph test:

**Paragraph 1 (simple):**
> "Would you like to make $10,000 a month making your own ice cream? My wife Lindsay and I do. In fact, sometimes we make thousands more. We showed our friend Steve, and now he makes an additional $4,300 every month with ease. Then keep reading. Because by the time you're finished this letter, you'll know how. In fact, I'm going to spill the beans and tell you over 48 insider secrets that not one person in 1,000 knows. Any one of these secrets is worth the cost of this entire package."

**Paragraph 2 (complex):**
> "If you wish to acquire vast financial resources, please pay close attention to the following information. Numerous individuals in the frozen confectionary industry have, for years, kept closely guarded secrets that reveal the fast route to establishing yourself as a much-in-demand artisan ice cream producer in a ridiculously short period of time."

The stats:

| Paragraph | Sentences/para | Words/sentence | Chars/word | Flesch ease | Grade level |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (simple) | 7.0 | 13.1 | 4.1 | 72.1 | 6.4 |
| 2 (complex) | 3.0 | 25.3 | 5.3 | 34.1 | 14.7 |

Paragraph 1 reads at sixth-grade level — accessible, compelling. Paragraph 2 reads at college-graduate level — same content, far fewer readers willing to engage.

### The four prescriptions

#### Prescription 1 — Use short, simple words

Plain. Direct. Conversational. *Get* > *acquire*. *Show* > *demonstrate*. *Buy* > *purchase*. *Use* > *utilize*. The fancy synonym always loses.

Specificity matters too:

| Don't say | Do say |
|---|---|
| Become financially successful | You'll make up to $2,495 every week |
| Want your entire body to look more appealing? | MEN! Do you want a rippling, rock-hard stomach? WOMEN! Do you want lean, luscious thighs? |

#### Prescription 2 — The shorter your sentences, the better

**Express only one thought per sentence.** Use the next sentence for the next thought. Why? Because readers process one thought at a time. And every thought you bury in a long sentence is one thought less likely to land.

#### Prescription 3 — Short, short paragraphs

Two-, three-, four-word paragraphs hit hard. They reset the reader's eye, accelerate the pace, and emphasize what's in them.

> "I confess."
> "We're free."
> "It's tough."
> "This is important."
> "S.W.A.T. teams use them."

Long copy doesn't mean dense copy. It means many short paragraphs.

#### Prescription 4 — Pile on personal pronouns

Load with **you, me, I, he, she, him, they, them**. Especially generous with **you** and **I**.

> "Do you... Can you... Would you... Should you...
> May I ask you a question?
> May I get your opinion?
> Did you know...
> Let me tell you...
> I think you'll like this idea..."

The personal pronouns make copy feel like a conversation between two people, not a brand pronouncement at a crowd.

**Don't write this — formal, academic, pronoun-poor:**

```text
"It is widely understood that contemporary consumers seek
products which provide comprehensive solutions to their
day-to-day operational challenges, and which can demonstrably
improve productivity outcomes through automation of
non-value-adding tasks."
```

(One sentence. 41 words. Zero "you" or "I". College-graduate-level.)

**Write this instead — simple, short, pronoun-rich:**

```text
"You're losing 45 minutes a day to busywork.

That's 4 hours a week. 200 hours a year. Five full work weeks
spent on tasks a tool could do in seconds.

Here's the tool. Here's how it works. Here's what you'll do
with the time you get back.

I built it because I had the same problem."
```

(Multiple sentences, multiple paragraphs, pronoun-dense. Grade-school readable. Same message, far higher conversion.)

### Common mistakes

- **Mistaking sophistication for simplicity** — "the simple version" doesn't mean dumbing down ideas; it means clearing the language.
- **Long sentences with multiple clauses** — split them.
- **Wall-of-text paragraphs** — break them every few sentences.
- **Avoiding "I" because it feels self-centered** — "I" makes it human; "we" or "the company" makes it corporate.
- **Avoiding "you" to be polite** — every "you" is engagement; every "users / customers / people" is distance.
- **Using jargon to sound expert** — clarity sounds more expert than jargon.

### How to test simplicity

- Read it aloud. Anywhere you stumble, the reader will too.
- Run it through a Flesch-Kincaid checker. Aim for grade 6-8 unless the audience explicitly demands higher.
- Count "you" / "I" frequency. If you can't find them, the copy is corporate.
- Hand it to someone outside your industry. Note where they pause or look confused.

### When to apply

- All direct-response copy.
- All email.
- All advertising.
- Most landing pages.

### When NOT to apply

- Highly technical B2B docs where the audience expects domain-specific language.
- Compliance / legal language where precision is mandated.
- Very high-end luxury where deliberate restraint and elevated language is part of the positioning. (Even there, simplicity wins more than copywriters expect.)
