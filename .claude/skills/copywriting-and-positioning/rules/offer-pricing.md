## Choose odd-cent pricing for value, round-number pricing for prestige

**Impact: MEDIUM** — Two competing pricing-format theories, both empirically supported, applied to different positioning. Pick deliberately.

**Odd-Even Pricing (value signal):**
Prices ending in odd amounts — 77, 95, 99 — suggest greater value than the next round dollar. **$9.77 reads as a better deal than $10.00.** The brain anchors on the leading digit; "$9.something" is in the $9 bucket, not the $10 bucket.

**Prestige Pricing (quality signal):**
Round whole numbers signal higher quality and intentional positioning. **$1,000.00 reads as higher-quality than $999.95** — because we've been culturally conditioned to associate fractional pricing with bargain-hunting, and round numbers with confidence and quality.

**The mismatch trap:** a luxury brand pricing at $1,997 reads cheaper than its $2,000 competitor. A value brand pricing at $19 reads more expensive than its $19.99 alternative. Format the price to match what you're selling.

**Don't write this — odd-cent price on a luxury product:**

```text
Premium Italian leather watch strap: $179.99
```

Why it fails: positioning is "premium / Italian leather / made in Florence." The .99 ending undercuts the prestige. Reads as "trying to seem premium but priced like a discount." Format-positioning mismatch.

**Write this instead — round price for prestige positioning:**

```text
Premium Italian leather watch strap: $180
```

Why it works: round number signals confidence, intentionality, prestige. Matches the rest of the positioning.

**The reverse case — value product, use odd cents:**

```text
DON'T (value positioning, prestige format):
Bulk USB cables, 10-pack: $20.00

DO (value positioning, value format):
Bulk USB cables, 10-pack: $19.97
```

The $19.97 reads as "best deal," which matches the value positioning. The $20.00 reads as "they didn't bother to discount."

### Other pricing-format moves

- **Amortize** the price for high-ticket items: "$3 a day" is psychologically smaller than "$1,095 a year," even though they're identical.
- **Boost the value** — show retail price, then your price: "$300 value, your price $97." (See `proof-evidence.md`.)
- **Diminish the price** — compare to a familiar small expense: "less than a cup of coffee a day."
- **Explain a low price** — "Our boss ordered too many, so this week only…" Reasoning increases believability of the discount.
- **Anchoring** — show the most-expensive option first. Subsequent options read as cheaper by contrast.
- **Decoy pricing** — a deliberately overpriced option that makes the target option look reasonable.

### Common mistakes

- **Format-positioning mismatch** — biggest single error. Audit every price.
- **Inconsistent format across pages** — homepage uses round numbers, pricing page uses .99. Pick one.
- **Free shipping threshold off by a small amount** — "Free shipping over $50" + a $48 cart is friction. Either lower the threshold or use psychology to bridge it.
- **Hiding the price** — "Contact us for pricing" works in some B2B contexts and fails in most. Default to showing the price.
- **Not testing** — pricing format is one of the easiest A/B tests with one of the largest impacts. Test if the volume supports it.

### When to apply

- Every transactional surface — every product page, every checkout, every offer.
- Pricing pages and tier comparisons.
- Email subject lines containing prices.
- Ad copy quoting prices.

### When NOT to apply

- N/A — every price has a format. The choice always matters.
