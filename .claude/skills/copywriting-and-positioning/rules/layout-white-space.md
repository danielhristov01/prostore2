## Wrap ads in white space — buy bigger, but don't fill it

**Impact: MEDIUM** — A counterintuitive lift: instead of filling a larger ad with more content, **buy more space but keep your original content the same size — surrounded by white space**. The white-space wrap creates visual isolation, and isolation is one of the strongest attention-grabbing patterns in print and digital.

### The Cashvertising data

| Composition | Relative attention |
|---|---|
| Standard half-page (filled with content) | 100% |
| Half-page with quarter-page ad in center, surrounded by white | **176%** |
| Standard full-page (filled with content) | 141% |

A white-wrapped half-page outperforms even a standard full-page. Same content, less of the page used — more attention.

### Why it works

- **Pattern interrupt** — the eye is trained to expect content fills the ad space. Empty space breaks the pattern.
- **Implied confidence** — "they don't need to fill it; they're confident enough to leave it empty."
- **Focus** — the eye lands on the smaller content faster because there's nothing competing for attention around it.
- **Premium signal** — luxury brands have used this for decades. White space is expensive (you're paying for empty space) and looks expensive.

### Where to apply

- **Print ads** — buy the larger size, leave the ring around it white.
- **Hero sections of landing pages** — generous padding around the headline + CTA, not edge-to-edge content.
- **Email marketing** — narrower content column, large vertical padding.
- **Display ads (digital)** — when you control the design, leave intentional empty space around the core ad.

**Don't write this — content packed edge-to-edge:**

```text
[Half-page ad, fully filled with text, multiple bullet points,
testimonials, product shots, CTAs, badges, all crowded into
every available pixel]
```

Why it fails: the eye doesn't know where to look. Each element competes with its neighbors. Attention diffuses across the noise.

**Write this instead — same content, framed by white:**

```text
[Half-page slot]

[Lots of white space at top]

       [Headline — large, centered]

       [One paragraph of body — short]

       [Single CTA button]

[Lots of white space at bottom]
```

Why it works:
- The eye locks on the ad as a *frame within a frame*.
- Less competing content within the ad means the core message lands.
- The visual relief stands out against neighboring (likely cluttered) content.

### Common mistakes

- **Filling every available pixel** — most ads do this. Don't.
- **White-space-only without strong content** — empty space alone isn't a strategy; it has to frame strong content.
- **Mistaking "tight margins" for "minimal"** — minimal is a deliberate compositional choice, not just smaller margins. The wrap is generous, not tight.
- **Buying bigger and adding more content** — defeats the point. Buy bigger, keep content small.
- **Inconsistent white space across surfaces** — branded materials should have consistent breathing rhythm.

### When to apply

- Display advertising in print, digital, and OOH.
- Hero sections of landing pages.
- Premium positioning generally.
- Anywhere "more space, less content" is purchasable.

### When NOT to apply

- Pages where every pixel is metered for response (some retail catalog pages, dense informational pages).
- When the budget genuinely cannot afford to "waste" space — though research suggests the lift typically outweighs the cost.
