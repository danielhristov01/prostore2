## Establish a Unique Selling Proposition the audience can't get from any competitor

**Impact: CRITICAL** — When the prospect can't tell you apart from competitors, they have no reason to prefer you, so they default to whichever is cheapest or closest. Without a USP, every other rule in this skill is patching a leaky boat. Define the USP first.

A USP names something the prospect can buy from *you* that they cannot get the same way from anyone else. It's not a slogan, not a brand line, not "high quality, great service." It's a specific, articulable, defensible claim.

The Nifty Fifty's diner example: they don't say "great burgers." They say:

- We never use frozen beef.
- Our hamburger beef is ground fresh daily.
- French fries are fresh-cut and homemade.
- Onion rings are not frozen — see the man dipping them in batter through the kitchen window.
- Here's the oil we fry in.
- Here's why eating here beats Burger Slop down the block.

Each sentence is a specific, verifiable, harder-than-it-looks claim. Together they form a USP — "freshly made fast food made visible." Competitors *could* match it, but mostly don't.

**Two layers of USP:**

1. **Brand USP** — what the company is. ("The agency that only takes on three clients a year.")
2. **Offer USP** — what *this offer* is. ("19-Year-Old College Kid Discovers Clever Way to Persuade People to Send You Money Via PayPal.")

Both should be in the copy. The brand USP differentiates the company; the offer USP differentiates the specific pitch.

**Don't write this — generic value claim that any competitor could make:**

```text
Headline: Work from home and turn your email into big profits!

Body: Our system teaches you proven email marketing techniques.
Top quality content. Trusted by thousands. Get started today.
```

Why it fails: zero unique claim. "Proven techniques," "top quality," "trusted by thousands" — every competitor says this. Reader has no reason to prefer.

**Write this instead — specific, unique, undefendable-by-competitors claim:**

```text
Headline: 19-Year-Old College Kid Discovers Clever Way to
Persuade People to Send You Money via PayPal.

Body: I'm Jake. Two summers ago I was broke. Last summer I made
$184,000 in 90 days using a single 4-paragraph email format
nobody else is using. This page shows you the exact format.
You'll also see a video of me writing one in real time, the 11
mistakes I made before it worked, and the $47 software stack
I'm running it on.
```

Why it works:
- **Specific person** (19-year-old, name, college, broke-then-rich arc) — competitors can't copy.
- **Specific result** ($184K, 90 days) — falsifiable, credible-feeling.
- **Specific method** ("single 4-paragraph email format") — unique mechanism.
- **Specific stack** ($47 in software) — concrete differentiator.
- A competitor would have to *be a different person* to match this offer.

### How to find the USP

Ask:

1. What can you say about your product/service that's *true*, *specific*, *important to the buyer*, and that *no competitor easily says*?
2. What do you *do* that competitors don't? (Process, materials, people, guarantees, speed, attention.)
3. What do you *not* do? Often a USP is a deliberate omission — "no contracts," "no upsells," "no offshore support."
4. Who is your customer that competitors can't serve as well? (Niche specialization is itself a USP.)

If the answer to all four is "nothing distinctive" — the USP work hasn't been done. Stop writing copy until it has.

### Common mistakes

- **Generic USPs** — "high quality, fair price, friendly service" is what every weak business says. Specific or nothing.
- **Vague differentiation** — "the trusted leader in [category]" is a slogan, not a USP. Slogans aren't USPs.
- **Fragile USPs** — "the only company doing X" only holds until a competitor does X. Better USPs are built on durable difference (people, process, position, philosophy).
- **No USP under positioning** — if you can't articulate the USP, the positioning doesn't exist.
- **Reading as braggadocio** — Nifty Fifty's didn't brag, they specified. "We never use frozen beef" is a fact; "we're the best!" is a brag. Specific facts > general brags.

### When to apply

- Brand positioning work — first.
- Every landing page — the USP appears in the hero.
- Every offer-specific page — has its own offer-USP under the brand USP.
- Anywhere a prospect could substitute a competitor and not notice the difference (which means: everywhere).

### When NOT to apply

- N/A. Every persuasive piece needs a USP foundation. The rule always applies.
