## Borrow credibility by associating the product with respected symbols, people, or institutions

**Impact: HIGH** — When a prospect doesn't yet trust your brand, an authority symbol short-circuits the trust-building process. The Institute for Propaganda Analysis showed the lab-coat effect decades ago: viewers transfer trust in physicians onto products endorsed by lab-coated figures, instantly.

The mechanism: the prospect's existing positive association with a respected entity (doctor, scientist, university, prestigious brand, celebrity, certification body) gets attached to your product when you visually or verbally pair them. The brain doesn't audit the logic — it just feels "this is endorsed."

This is distinct from `proof-social-proof.md` (which uses *peers*) and `psych-weapons-of-influence.md` (where authority is one of six levers). Transfer is specifically about **borrowed symbolic credibility** — the prop, the logo, the name-drop, the setting.

**Don't write this — naked claim with no credibility anchor:**

```text
Headline: Our pain reliever works fast.

Body: Take two and feel relief in 30 minutes. Available at most
pharmacies.
```

Why it fails: pure self-claim. Reader has no reason to believe "fast." No borrowed authority, no transferred trust.

**Write this instead — anchor the claim to a credibility symbol:**

```text
Headline: Recommended by the doctors at three of the top five
pain clinics in the country.

Body: Tested in the same labs that test prescription analgesics.
Cleared for over-the-counter use because it works as fast as
prescription-strength relief — without the prescription. Two
tablets. 30 minutes. Pharmacist-approved.
```

Why it works:
- **Pain clinics** → transfers medical authority.
- **Same labs as prescription analgesics** → transfers regulatory rigor.
- **Pharmacist-approved** → transfers retail-channel trust.
- The functional claim ("30 minutes") is identical — but it's now wrapped in three layers of borrowed credibility.

### Common mistakes

- **False or unearned associations** — claiming endorsements you don't have isn't transfer, it's fraud. Stay accurate.
- **Stale or weak symbols** — outdated logos, unrecognizable institutions, or D-list celebrities can transfer *negative* signal. Choose symbols the audience actually recognizes and respects.
- **Symbol mismatch with audience** — a Harvard logo means nothing to a tradesperson; a "trusted by 50,000 contractors" line means nothing to a Harvard MBA. Match the symbol to the buyer's hierarchy of respect.
- **Burying the symbol** — transfer happens at first glance. Logo/name in body copy paragraph 4 is wasted; put it where the eye lands.

### When to apply

- New brand or unknown vendor — borrow trust until you build your own.
- Categories where stakes are high (health, finance, safety, B2B contracts).
- When you have legitimate associations: certifications, partner logos, customer logos, expert endorsers, media mentions.

### When NOT to apply

- Audience is anti-authority by disposition (some communities react against "experts say"). Use peer/social proof instead.
- The borrowed symbol clashes with the brand voice (irreverent challenger brands name-dropping institutions can read as insecure).
- You're already the authority — transferring from someone else can demote you.
