## Put the biggest benefit in the headline — without exception

**Impact: CRITICAL** — *"Unless your headline sells your product, you have wasted 90 percent of your money."* — David Ogilvy.

The reader is exposed to 247–3,000+ ads per day. Every headline is competing not against your competitors but against attention itself. The single highest-leverage decision in the entire piece of copy is what goes in the headline. The biggest, most-resonant **benefit** the product delivers must appear there.

Two rules in priority order:

1. **Always put your biggest benefit in your headline.** No exceptions.
2. **If two equally strong headlines exist, the shorter one will likely get more readership.** All other things equal, shorter wins. But never trade rule #1 for rule #2.

Note: "biggest benefit" means the LF8-laddered benefit (`psych-means-end-chain.md`), not a feature, not a slogan, not a brand line. If your headline could run on a competitor's product unchanged, it's wrong.

**Don't write this — feature, brand, or vague-benefit headline:**

```text
Headline: Quality Italian leather goods since 1962.
```

Why it fails: this is a brand statement, not a benefit. Reader has no reason to read on. Could be on any leather brand's site. No LF8 lever, no benefit, no specificity.

```text
Headline: The world's best running shoe.
```

Why it fails: vague benefit, unbacked superlative. "Best" is unfalsifiable, so the brain discounts it. No specific benefit to the reader.

**Write this instead — biggest, specific, LF8-laddered benefit:**

```text
Headline: How a 73-year-old realtor closed 31 listings last year
without leaving her neighborhood.
```

Why it works:
- Names the **biggest benefit** for the audience (more closings, less effort).
- Specific (73, 31, "neighborhood") → triggers length-implies-strength heuristic.
- Implicit LF8 (want #6 — be superior; want #5 — comfortable living conditions).
- Demands you read the body to find out how.

```text
Headline: Cleans grease off the stove in 8 seconds — without
scrubbing.
```

Why it works:
- The benefit (8 seconds, no scrubbing) IS the headline.
- Specific time (8 seconds) is more credible than "fast."
- "Without scrubbing" tells you the threshold of effort it removes.

### Common mistakes

- **Brand-led headlines** — "[Company name] is excellent" is not a headline. The reader doesn't care about your company until they care about what you do for them.
- **Cleverness over clarity** — see `body-cleverectomy.md`. Wordplay, alliteration, puns burn the headline slot on entertainment instead of selling.
- **Feature in the headline** — "4HP motor" is a feature; "Treadmill that doesn't lurch when you sprint" is a benefit.
- **Multiple benefits stacked** — pick ONE. The biggest one. Stacking dilutes.
- **Vague superlatives** — "best," "amazing," "revolutionary" all read as marketing fluff. Specific > superlative.
- **Hidden benefit** — the benefit is somewhere in the ad but not the headline. Move it up.

### Diagnostic — is your headline strong enough?

- Could it run unchanged on a competitor's product? → Yes = weak.
- Does it name a specific benefit the reader values? → No = weak.
- Does it ladder to an LF8 want? → No = weak.
- Would it survive being read alone, with no body copy beneath it, as the entire ad? → No = weak.
- Is the benefit specific (numbers, named outcomes)? → No = weak.

### When to apply

- Every piece of direct-response copy. Always.
- Hero sections of landing pages.
- Email subject lines (same rule applies).
- Cold ads, retargeting ads, sales pages.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure brand awareness work where no benefit needs to land (rare and expensive — most "brand work" still benefits from a benefit-led headline).
