## Translate every feature into a benefit, then pile them on

**Impact: CRITICAL** — The reader's silent question, every second of reading: **"What's in it for me?" (WIIFM).** Features answer the wrong question — they answer "what is this thing?" Benefits answer the right question — "what does it do for me?"

The translation pattern: *Feature → Benefit → LF8 want.* Run every feature through both translations. The Cashvertising example:

| Feature (what it IS) | Benefit (what it DOES for you) |
|---|---|
| Top-grade hand-selected seat leathers | Luxurious comfort in all climates |
| Deep-pile Wilton lamb's wool carpeting | Rich softness underfoot, lush elegance |
| 453hp 6.75L V-12 engine | Power, control, supreme reliability |
| Bold dignified styling, "Spirit of Ecstasy" hood ornament | The feeling of power, success, that you've "arrived" |

The feature is the same in any spec sheet. The benefit lands the LF8 want.

After translating each feature → benefit, **pile them on**. The Length-Implies-Strength heuristic (`psych-heuristics.md`) means more benefits = stronger product perception. Don't ration; lay them all out.

**Don't write this — feature stack with no translation:**

```text
Inkjet Printer X1:
- 4 ink tanks
- 500-sheet tray
- Ultra-Draft mode
- USB and WiFi
- Auto duplex
- 4800x1200 dpi
```

Why it fails: each line answers "what is it?" not "what does it do for me?" Reader gets data without desire.

**Write this instead — features translated to benefits, piled on:**

```text
Why people switch to the X1:

- Four separate ink tanks. Replace only the color that runs out
  — instead of throwing away a whole cartridge with usable ink
  in three colors. Saves about $80 a year for the average user.

- 500-sheet tray. Fill it once and forget about it for two
  months. Most printers hold half as much; you'll fill them
  twice as often.

- Ultra-Draft mode uses 50% less ink than standard draft mode
  on competing printers. Print the throwaway pages — drafts,
  receipts, internal docs — at a fraction of the cost.

- USB and WiFi both. Plug in your laptop, or send from your
  phone, or have your kid print homework from down the hall.
  Whatever's most convenient that day.

- Automatic duplex (prints both sides without you flipping the
  paper). Saves paper, saves trees, saves you the trip back to
  the printer mid-job.

- 4800×1200 dpi resolution. Your photos look like real prints,
  not "okay-for-a-home-printer" prints. Photo paper makes them
  gallery-quality.
```

Why it works:
- Every feature is translated to a benefit (`psych-means-end-chain.md`).
- Specific numbers ($80/year, 50%, 4800×1200) — credibility.
- Piling effect (`psych-heuristics.md`) — six benefits look more complete than six features.
- Each benefit ladders to an LF8 (economy, convenience, family, work).

### The Cashvertising mantra

> "Tell me quick and tell me true, or else, dear friend, the hell with you... Not how this product came to be, But what the damn thing does for me!" — Anonymous

Make this the inner monologue you assume your reader is having. Every line of body copy must survive the WIIFM test.

### Common mistakes

- **Feature lists with no translation** — most product pages.
- **Translating features without laddering up** — "saves you 30 seconds" is benefit, not LF8 destination. Push further: "30 seconds saved every time you do X = 4 hours back per year = a Saturday afternoon with the family."
- **Counting features as differentiators when competitors have the same features** — translate to benefits, but also identify which benefits competitors *can't* match (USP — see `offer-usp.md`).
- **Stopping at one or two benefits** — pile on. Length-implies-strength.
- **Stacking too many benefits without prioritization** — lead with the biggest one (`headline-biggest-benefit.md`), follow with the rest in descending order of pull.

### When to apply

- Every product description page.
- Every feature list.
- Hero copy translation.
- Pricing/tier comparison copy.
- Spec sheets that are also marketing copy.

### When NOT to apply

- Engineering specs documents where features ARE the answer (datasheets, RFP responses for technical buyers).
- Pure brand voice work where benefit-listing reads off-tone (rare, but exists in luxury and category-defining brand campaigns).
