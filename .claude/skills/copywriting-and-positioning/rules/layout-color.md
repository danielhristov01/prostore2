## Color affects perceived weight, taste, and brand association — pick deliberately, not aesthetically

**Impact: MEDIUM** — Color isn't just preference (`layout-color-preferences.md`); it actively changes how the buyer *perceives* the product. Two well-documented effects:

### Effect 1: Color affects perceived weight ("apparent weight")

Darker colors are perceived as heavier. Lighter colors as lighter. Same physical object — package, button, product — *feels* heavier when colored darker.

- Dumbbells in matte black look heavier than dumbbells in chrome.
- A black laptop feels more "premium / substantial" than a white one of the same weight.
- Light packaging signals "easy / casual / portable"; dark signals "serious / weighty / important."

Pick the apparent-weight signal that matches positioning. Heavy duty? Go dark. Light and breezy? Go light.

### Effect 2: Color affects taste perception and brand association

Color carries category associations so strong that swapping colors can rewrite which product the brain thinks it's seeing.

The Cashvertising example: **Canada Dry sugar-free ginger ale** ran in red-and-white packaging. Berni Corp. designers changed it to **green-and-white**. **Sales jumped 25%+.**

Why? Red is "owned" by Coca-Cola in soft drinks. Consumers seeing red cans on a shelf had been unconsciously processing "cola." A green-and-white ginger ale can broke the cola cue and let the product be seen for what it actually was.

### What this means for your color choices

- **Audit your category's color "owners."** Every category has dominant brands that own colors. Coca-Cola owns red in soda. McDonald's owns red+yellow in fast food. Tiffany owns robin's-egg blue in jewelry. UPS owns brown in shipping. *If you're not the owner, picking the owned color confuses buyers into thinking you're the owner — which usually fails to differentiate and may misposition.*
- **Pick for the perception, not the prettiness.** What do you want the buyer to FEEL about the product? Heavy / light? Cheap / premium? Sweet / sour? Choose colors that match.
- **Test color shifts** with real sample sizes. The Canada Dry case shows shifts can be large; don't assume your intuition predicts them.

### Cultural variability

Color associations are partly cultural. Red is luck/celebration in much of East Asia, danger/warning in much of Europe and North America. White is purity/weddings in the West, mourning in parts of East Asia. Black, in most contexts, signals premium/luxury — but in some specifically signals death.

If you're operating across cultures, audit color choices per market.

### Common mistakes

- **Picking colors aesthetically** — "I like this teal" is the wrong basis for a $50K rebrand. Position first, color second.
- **Ignoring category color owners** — see Canada Dry / Coke. Map who owns what before choosing.
- **Same color across products that should differentiate** — flagship and budget tier in the same color confuses positioning.
- **Forgetting apparent-weight signal** — a luxury product in cheap-bright colors fails to project premium.
- **Cross-cultural blunders** — single-market color choices applied globally without audit.

### When to apply

- Brand identity decisions.
- Packaging design.
- Repositioning campaigns.
- Product line differentiation (premium vs. value vs. bulk).
- Diagnosing why a brand "feels off" — color mismatch with positioning is a frequent cause.

### When NOT to apply

- When category convention so strongly dictates a color that defying it would lose recognition (e.g., yellow taxis, red fire trucks).
- When brand systems already exist — modify only with strong evidence.
