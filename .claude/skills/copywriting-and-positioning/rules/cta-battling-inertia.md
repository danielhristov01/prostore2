## Push for action — make it easy to act, then explicitly ask

**Impact: CRITICAL** — *"Advertising is selling in print."* — Daniel Starch. A salesperson who informs but doesn't close is a bad salesperson. An ad that informs but doesn't push for action is a bad ad. Inertia — the human default to do nothing — beats every other force on the page if not actively fought.

The two-step formula:

1. **Make it easy to act.**
2. **Explicitly ask for action.**

Both are required. Friction-free with no ask = passive. Asking with friction = blocked. Together = response.

### Step 1: Make it easy to act

Strip every avoidable obstacle between *intent* and *outcome*:

- **Visible phone number** — large, accompanied by "It's easy to order."
- **Accept every channel** — phone, mail, online, app. The customer picks; you don't impose.
- **Long, strong guarantee** (`offer-guarantees.md`) — removes loss fear.
- **Installment / financing** for items >$15 — boosts response by ~15%.
- **No-friction return policy.**
- **One-click checkout** — pre-filled forms, saved cards, single-step.
- **Specific, simple instructions**: "Clip this coupon. Bring it to our store by August 21. Save 50%."
- **Free shipping** — removes "but the shipping cost…"
- **"Bill Me" or credit option** — boosts response 50%+ in some categories.
- **Free gift for fast reply** — a layered scarcity-plus-reciprocation.

Each removed friction point is a measurable conversion lift.

### Step 2: Ask explicitly

The reader should never have to figure out what to do next. Tell them:

- **Order Now**
- **Call before [date]**
- **Click below to start your free trial**
- **Reply YES to this email**
- **Bring this coupon to our store**

Specificity matters. "Order Now" beats "Get started." "Call 555-1234" beats "Get in touch." "Reply YES" beats "Let me know if you're interested."

**Don't write this — informative ad with no call to action:**

```text
Body: We've been making artisan ice cream for 14 years. We use
local cream, organic sugar, and fresh fruit. Our shop is open
Monday-Saturday, 11-9.

Address: 123 Main Street.
```

Why it fails: zero ask. Reader gets information, says "neat," moves on. Inertia wins.

**Write this instead — easy to act + explicit ask + scarcity:**

```text
Body: Free scoop of any flavor — just bring this email to the
shop by Saturday, June 8.

No purchase necessary. No gimmick. We want you to taste why
people drive 30 minutes for our salted caramel.

The fine print: one free scoop per email per person. Email must
be shown on a phone or printed out. Open Mon-Sat 11am-9pm.
123 Main Street, Chicago.

Bring this email. Get a scoop. We think you'll come back.

— Tomas, owner
```

Why it works:
- **Easy to act** — no purchase required, just walk in.
- **Explicit ask** — "bring this email," "get a scoop."
- **Specific instructions** — when, where, how.
- **Scarcity** — by Saturday.
- **Reciprocation** (`psych-weapons-of-influence.md`) — they got something free, they'll feel obligated to come back.
- **Inertia is removed** by making the next step concrete and trivial.

### The Cashvertising Action checklist

A condensed list of action-stage tactics worth running through every direct-response piece:

- ☐ Phone number large and visible, with "It's easy to order"
- ☐ Multiple ordering channels (phone, mail, online)
- ☐ Long strong guarantee — longer than competitors' (`offer-guarantees.md`)
- ☐ Installment payments for >$15 items
- ☐ "Order Now" or equivalent direct ask appears multiple times
- ☐ Coupon or response device included
- ☐ Deadline set (`offer-scarcity.md`)
- ☐ Free gift / bonus for fast response
- ☐ Free shipping (or threshold disclosed)
- ☐ "Bill Me" / "Pay later" option where applicable
- ☐ Return policy / risk reversal stated near the CTA
- ☐ Simple, specific instructions ("1. Click. 2. Enter email. 3. Done.")

### Common mistakes

- **Soft CTAs** — "Learn more," "Get in touch," "Find out more" all defer action. Use them only when the next page is itself a direct-response page.
- **Single CTA on a long page** — long pages should have CTAs at multiple points (top, mid, bottom, after objections, after testimonials).
- **CTA without a "why now"** — pair every action ask with scarcity (`offer-scarcity.md`) or a fresh benefit.
- **Asking before earning the right** — CTAs in cold copy before benefit/proof have been delivered convert poorly. AIDA sequence: Attention → Interest → Desire → THEN Action.
- **"Click here"** as the only button text — vague. "Start my 14-day trial" specifies the action AND the value AND the risk profile.

### The Hopkins/Schwab principle

> *"A salesman does not say, 'How do you do?' speak a few words about his product, then ask you to sign the order. No, he uses enough words to get your emotions and reasoning power flowing toward a sale."* — Victor Schwab

The CTA isn't an ask in isolation. It's the close at the end of a built case. AIDA sequence applies — make sure desire was built before action is asked.

### When to apply

- Every direct-response piece, every time.
- Every email in a persuasive sequence.
- Every product page.
- Every ad.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure top-of-funnel awareness content where action isn't yet appropriate (rare — even there, a soft CTA to subscribe / follow / read more keeps the funnel moving).
