## Set headlines in initial caps; serif for print body, sans-serif for web body

**Impact: MEDIUM** — Three rules with strong empirical backing across decades of readability research:

### Rule 1 — Headline casing

- **ALL CAPS** for very short headlines (about 4-5 words or fewer). Beyond that, ALL CAPS slows reading dramatically and reduces comprehension.
- **Initial Caps** for any headline longer than a couple of words. ("The Quick Brown Fox Jumps Over Lazy Dogs.")
- **Sentence case** is the modern web alternative to initial caps; both work, but initial caps still tests slightly stronger for headline impact in most direct-response contexts.

ALL CAPS reads ~13-20% slower because each letter has roughly the same shape (rectangular) — the eye loses the ascender/descender pattern it uses to skim. Initial caps preserves the pattern.

### Rule 2 — Print body copy → serif

For ink on paper (newspapers, magazines, sales letters, brochures, direct mail):

- **Serif fonts read easier** at body-text size. Findings consistent across virtually every readability study since the 1930s.
- Recommended print body faces: Schoolbook, Garamond, Caslon, Georgia, Times New Roman.
- This is why every newspaper and book in history defaults to serif for body.

### Rule 3 — Online body copy → sans-serif (mostly)

For screens:

- **Arial** for 12-point text and larger.
- **Verdana** for smaller than 12-point (rarely go smaller than 10-point).
- **Georgia** if you want a more formal/serif feel online and still need readability — Georgia was specifically designed for screen.
- **For older readers, use 14-point** as the floor.

Modern web typography has more options (system fonts, custom web fonts), but the core rule holds: at small screen sizes, sans-serif reads more cleanly. At large sizes (large landing-page paragraphs, blog posts), web-optimized serifs can match or beat sans-serif.

**Don't write this — ALL CAPS long headline + serif on a small-screen web body:**

```text
Headline (32pt): IF YOU WANT TO LOSE WEIGHT WITHOUT GIVING UP
ALL THE FOODS YOU LOVE THIS PROGRAM IS DESIGNED FOR YOU

Body (10pt Times New Roman, on a phone screen):
[a paragraph of body copy at 10pt serif on a 6-inch screen]
```

Why it fails: ALL CAPS in a long headline cuts reading speed and looks shouty. 10pt Times on a phone is small and serif strokes break up at low pixel densities — readers squint, then bounce.

**Write this instead — initial caps headline + sans-serif body for web:**

```text
Headline (36pt, Initial Caps):
"How to Lose Weight Without Giving Up the Foods You Love"

Body (16pt Arial, on phone):
"Most diets fail because they ask you to eat foods you hate.
This one works the opposite way…"
```

Why it works:
- Initial caps preserves reading pattern.
- 16pt sans-serif is comfortable on small screens.
- Both choices remove friction the reader otherwise pays in micro-fatigue.

### Other typography rules worth knowing

- **Quote marks around a headline can boost readership ~25%.** They visually signal "someone said this," which engages curiosity. ("Most home treadmills will break in 2 years. Here's the one that won't.")
- **Body copy should be 10-14pt** for print, 14-18pt for web (16pt has become the modern default for body).
- **Line length 50-75 characters** — wider becomes hard to track between lines; narrower (mobile) is fine.
- **Use 1.4-1.6× line height** — tighter looks cramped; looser breaks paragraph cohesion.
- **No more than 2-3 typefaces per ad/page** (one for headlines, one for body, optionally one for accents). Three max.

### Common mistakes

- **ALL CAPS body copy** — drops reading speed by 20%+ AND looks shouty.
- **Mixing too many faces** — every section a different font. Pick one or two and stick.
- **Tiny mobile body text** — 10-12pt for body on a phone is hostile to readers over 35.
- **Decorative fonts in body** — script, blackletter, hand-drawn — break readability for any meaningful text length.
- **Color contrast failures** — light gray on white "looks elegant" but tests show readers skip what they can't easily read. Black-on-white or near-black-on-near-white for body, period.
- **Reverse type** (light text on dark background) for long body copy — slower to read, harder on the eye over paragraphs. (Single short headlines and CTA buttons reverse fine.)

### When to apply

- Every printed piece, every web page, every email.
- Always audit existing materials against these defaults; many old templates violate them.

### When NOT to apply

- Brand designers will sometimes break these rules deliberately for art-direction reasons. That's fine for hero/brand moments. Don't break them for body copy that needs to be read.
