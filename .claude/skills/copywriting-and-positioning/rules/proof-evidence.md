## Back every claim with evidence — facts, figures, testimonials, studies — that you didn't create yourself

**Impact: CRITICAL** — People buy when they believe the product is worth more than what they're trading. They will *want* to believe your benefit claims (so they can enjoy the benefits), but they're also afraid of being wrong (loss aversion). Evidence resolves the conflict — it gives them permission to believe.

The "what's in the envelope?" thought experiment: nobody trades $20 for an unknown envelope, but everyone trades $1 for a visible $20 bill. Evidence is what makes the value visible — it's the bag through which the prospect can see the bill.

Evidence types, roughly in order of strength:

1. **Independent third-party data** — research studies, government reports, ratings from trusted bodies. Strongest because you didn't make them.
2. **Named, photographed, specific testimonials** — full name, city, photo, specific outcome with numbers. (See `proof-social-proof.md`.)
3. **Customer count + scale signals** — "12,000 dentists," "shipped 4M units," "active in 47 countries."
4. **Demonstration** — video or image of the product working, especially against a stress test.
5. **Specifics with verifiable numbers** — "saves you 23.4 minutes per workflow" beats "saves time."
6. **Named partners / customers / press** — logos of companies, publications, certifications.
7. **Generic testimonials / vague claims** — weakest. Better than nothing, barely.

The bar: **evidence must originate outside your marketing department.** Self-made evidence is barely evidence; it's a claim wearing evidence costume.

**Don't write this — claim without evidence:**

```text
Headline: The fastest pain reliever on the market.

Body: Take two and feel relief in just 30 minutes. Doctors love
it. Try it today!
```

Why it fails:
- "Fastest" — unbacked superlative.
- "30 minutes" — claim, no source.
- "Doctors love it" — vague, plural, anonymous, no number.
- Reader can't verify any of it. The brain discounts to ~zero.

**Write this instead — every claim anchored to specific external evidence:**

```text
Headline: 30-minute relief — in a 2024 head-to-head trial against
Tylenol and Advil at p<0.01 significance.

Body: Trial conducted by [University Name] Pain Research Center,
funded by [neutral grant body], published in the Journal of
Analgesic Medicine, March 2024 (PMID: 12345678). 312 participants,
randomized, double-blind. Median time-to-50%-pain-reduction:

- Our product: 28 minutes
- Tylenol: 41 minutes
- Advil: 38 minutes

Endorsed by the American Pain Foundation. Recommended by 4 of
the top 5 pain clinics in the US (full list at the bottom of
this page). 92% would recommend, n=8,427 verified buyers, last
12 months.
```

Why it works:
- **Specific source** (university name, journal, PMID).
- **Methodology disclosed** (n, randomized, double-blind).
- **Specific numbers** (28 min, 41 min, 38 min) — falsifiable, therefore credible.
- **Multiple evidence types stacked** (trial, endorsement, clinic list, customer survey).
- **Verifiability** — a skeptical reader can check.

### Common mistakes

- **Generic testimonials** — "Great product! Highly recommend!" — Sarah K. is barely evidence. Specific outcome + photo + name + city is.
- **Vague superlatives** — "best," "fastest," "most reliable" without numbers and source.
- **Self-made "studies"** — internal surveys presented as research. Audience can tell.
- **Too few evidence points** — one testimonial below a hero is weaker than fifty. Length-implies-strength applies (`psych-heuristics.md`).
- **Evidence that doesn't match the claim** — "Trusted by 10,000 customers!" doesn't prove "saves you 30%." Match each claim to specific supporting evidence.
- **Making it the prospect's job to find the evidence** — link the source directly. Reader who has to dig won't.

### Two-bag demo (Cashvertising's example)

The exercise: hold up an unmarked envelope, ask audience for $20 in trade. Nobody trades. Hold up a clear bag with a $20 bill visible, ask for $1. Many trade.

**The lesson: visibility of value drives action.** Evidence is the clear bag. Without it, the prospect won't reach for the wallet — even when the deal is good.

### When to apply

- Every benefit claim. Every claim. No exceptions in direct-response.
- Especially when claims sound too-good-to-be-true (which strong claims always do — that's what makes them strong).
- High-ticket purchases where the buyer needs to justify the spend internally (to themselves, partner, boss, board).
- Skeptical audiences (engineers, doctors, journalists, procurement professionals).

### When NOT to apply

- Pure peripheral-route emotional copy where central-route audit isn't happening (impulse beverages, snacks). Even there, light evidence (ratings, "America's #1") still helps.
- When the evidence weakens the claim — if your "evidence" is a 12-person internal survey, don't cite it; either get better evidence or drop the claim.
