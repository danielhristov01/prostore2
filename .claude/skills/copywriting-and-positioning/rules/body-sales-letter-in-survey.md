## Disguise a sales offer inside a customer survey to bypass ad-resistance

**Impact: MEDIUM** — A pure sales email triggers ad-defense. A survey from a company a customer has done business with feels like *the company listening*, not selling. The customer engages with low resistance. The offer at the bottom of the survey then lands when the customer is in a cooperative, engaged frame — instead of a defensive, ad-skipping one.

This is also a stack of three Cialdini weapons (`psych-weapons-of-influence.md`):

- **Reciprocation** — the company values their input enough to ask.
- **Commitment / consistency** — they answered the questions, so they're already engaged with the company.
- **Liking** — being asked feels personal, raising affinity.

The structural template:

1. Short personal cover letter framing the survey as request-for-input.
2. 5-6 questions about the customer's experience (genuinely useful for product/marketing).
3. **At the bottom**, an offer framed as a thank-you — *only* available to survey respondents and *only* through a date.

### The Cashvertising template (auto-detailing example)

> *"Dear Eileen: May I ask you a favor? I'd like to know your honest opinion of our Deluxe Auto Detailing Service we performed for you recently. Would you kindly check off your answers to the questions below, and then mail this survey back to me in the postage-paid envelope enclosed? Thanks for being such a great customer. Sincerely…"*

Then 5 short questions. Then:

> *"Thanks, Eileen! To show my appreciation for completing this important survey, I'm sending you this 16-ounce bottle of Liquid Mirror Spray for 50% OFF to protect your car's just-detailed finish. Simply spray on and wipe off. It's regularly $10, but I'll send it to you — postage paid — for just $5 when you return this completed survey by [DATE]. This important product helps keep your car shining and seals out dirt for up to 50% longer when you apply it within 30 days of your detailing, so don't wait! HALF-PRICE SPECIAL AVAILABLE ONLY TO SURVEY RESPONDENTS!"*

Three things make this work:

- **Real survey questions** — not transparent decoration. Use the answers.
- **Genuine thank-you offer** — something you'd offer anyway, but framed as appreciation for participation.
- **Survey-respondent-only** + **deadline** — scarcity (`offer-scarcity.md`) without feeling promotional.

**Don't write this — naked sales pitch with no rapport-building:**

```text
Subject: Your car needs Liquid Mirror Spray!

Eileen, after detailing, your finish needs protection. Buy our
$10 spray today and keep your car shining. Order now!
```

Why it fails: pure pitch. Reader's ad-defense is on. Conversion is whatever the ad-skip rate is.

**Write this instead — survey wrapper with offer at the bottom:**

```text
Subject: Quick favor, Eileen?

Body:
Hi Eileen — Tomas here from [Detailing Co]. Quick favor, if
you have 90 seconds:

We finished your detail two weeks ago, and I wanted your honest
take on five things. Your answers help me train the new guys
and figure out what to invest in.

1. On a 1-10, how did the finish look when you picked it up?
2. Has the shine held up these past two weeks?
3. Anything we should have done that we didn't?
4. Anything we did that you didn't expect?
5. Would you tell a friend about us? (Yes/No)

[Reply button or link]

—

Tiny thank-you for filling this out: I'll ship you a 16oz bottle
of Liquid Mirror Spray (the same protective coating we use)
for $5 instead of the usual $10. It seals the finish for about
50% longer when you apply it within 30 days of detailing — so
this offer only makes sense for the next 16 days. After that
it's regular price.

Just check "yes, send me the spray" when you reply.

— Tomas
```

Why it works:
- **Survey is real** — genuine input, not manipulation theater.
- **Offer is contextual** — directly relevant to the service the customer just received.
- **Thank-you frame** — feels like appreciation, not pitch.
- **Scarcity is logical** (16-day window matches product effectiveness) — not arbitrary urgency.
- **Easy reply** — friction-light commitment.

### Common mistakes

- **Fake survey** — questions you'll never read or use. Customer can usually tell, and trust burns.
- **Wrong customer to survey** — don't survey customers who haven't experienced the product yet.
- **Offer disconnected from the service** — sells unrelated products under the survey wrapper. Reads as bait-and-switch.
- **No deadline / scarcity** — without it, the offer drifts. Always pair with a specific window.
- **Buried offer** — putting it too far below the survey, or after a too-long form. Survey should be short (5-6 questions); offer should follow immediately.

### When to apply

- Post-purchase / post-service follow-up emails.
- Customer retention sequences.
- Categories where natural follow-up products exist (auto, beauty, fitness, supplies, accessories).
- Annual / quarterly check-in cycles.

### When NOT to apply

- Cold acquisition (no rapport built; survey would feel weird).
- High-stakes B2B where survey-then-offer might read as flippant.
- Customers in dispute / unhappy. Surveys to unhappy customers should focus on resolution, not new offers.
