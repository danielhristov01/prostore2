## Stack Cialdini's six weapons of influence — comparison, liking, authority, reciprocation, commitment, scarcity

**Impact: CRITICAL** — These six are the most empirically validated peripheral-route levers in persuasion research. Use one, copy works. Stack two or three, copy compounds. Most strong direct-response pieces use four or more.

The six:

1. **Comparison (Social Proof / "everybody else is doing it")** — humans don't want to be left out. "12,000 dentists recommend X." "4.8 stars from 6,400 reviewers." See `proof-social-proof.md`.

2. **Liking (Balance Theory / "I like you, take my money")** — people buy from people they like and identify with. Show people who look like the prospect, or whom the prospect aspires to be. Pretty/familiar/similar → liking → buying.

3. **Authority** — official, intelligent, or authoritative figures save the prospect the work of evaluating. White lab coat. Doctor. Professor. "As featured in." See `psych-transfer-credibility.md`.

4. **Reciprocation** — when someone gives you something, social convention obligates a return gift. Offer something valuable for free up front (guide, sample, consultation, free shipping, lighter, tote, dollar-bill-in-the-survey-envelope). The prospect feels obligated. Even a 2-cent pen works.

5. **Commitment / Consistency ("Four Walls" technique)** — once a person commits to a small position, they remain consistent with it. Get a small yes first, then a larger one. The petition-then-donation example: sign petition (small commitment) → asked for $3 (consistency demands yes). Build copy as a chain of small agreements.

6. **Scarcity** — when something becomes harder to get, desire spikes. "Only 47 seats left." "Until Friday." "First 100 only." Loss-of-availability triggers ownership-feel before purchase. See `offer-scarcity.md`.

**Don't write this — single-weapon, no stacking:**

```text
Headline: Buy our online course today.

Body: It teaches you how to write copy. $497.
[Buy Now]
```

Why it fails: zero weapons engaged. No comparison, no liking, no authority, no reciprocation, no commitment, no scarcity. Pure assertion.

**Write this instead — stack four or more weapons:**

```text
Headline: How 4,200 founders learned to write copy that sells —
without hiring an agency.

Body: Hi, I'm [name]. I've written copy for Stripe, Vercel, and
the Y Combinator class of '23. [LIKING + AUTHORITY]

Last year I taught 4,200 founders the exact same playbook I use
for my agency clients — and 87% of them shipped a landing page
that out-converted their previous one within 30 days. [SOCIAL PROOF]

Free 12-page sample of the course: [download]. No email needed.
Just take it. [RECIPROCATION]

If after reading the sample you want the full course, the price
goes from $497 to $397 if you grab it before Friday — and only
the first 100 spots include the live group review call.
[SCARCITY]

Yes, I want the free sample. → [button]
[After clicking, prospect is in commitment chain — already
identified as someone interested in the course.] [COMMITMENT]
```

Six weapons engaged. Each one is doing its own work; together they compound.

### Common mistakes

- **Picking one weapon and stopping** — single-weapon copy is fine; multi-weapon copy is stronger. Stack on purpose.
- **Faking the weapon** — fake scarcity (perpetual countdown timers), fake authority (made-up credentials), fake social proof (planted reviews) all eventually break trust. Real weapons only.
- **Wrong weapon for the audience** — engineers respond well to authority and social proof from their tribe; less to celebrity liking. Match weapon to audience values.
- **Overloading low-stakes copy** — for a $7 product, stacking six weapons reads as desperate. Match weapon density to purchase weight.
- **Treating these as a checklist instead of a toolbox** — pick weapons that fit the product and brand voice. Forced reciprocation in a luxury brand reads cheap.

### When to apply

- Always have at least 2-3 weapons engaged in any direct-response piece.
- High-stakes purchases: stack 4+.
- Long-form sales pages: weapons should appear at every major section.
- Positioning statements: pick one or two weapons that the brand owns durably.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure information/awareness content where no purchase is being asked.
- Brand voice where heavy-handed influence reads off-tone (very high-end luxury, deeply trust-based B2B). Weapons still apply, but with restraint and elegance.
