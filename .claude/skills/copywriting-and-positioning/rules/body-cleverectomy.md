## Cut clever for clear — clever copy that doesn't sell is bad copy

**Impact: HIGH** — Most copy doesn't fail by being too plain. It fails by being too clever. Wordplay, alliteration, puns, in-jokes, abstract metaphors, and "cool" phrasings frequently look creative to the writer (and reviewers) while being incomprehensible or unmemorable to the prospect. The reader's job is not to admire your craft. Their job is to act on the offer — or move on.

The Cashvertising example: an agency was reviewed by an "expert" who said the headline *"Powerful Web Pages Designed in 24 Hours by Famous Marketing Expert for $199"* wasn't very creative.

The agency's response: read it again. That headline does several things at once:

- States exactly what's being sold.
- Capitalizes on instant gratification (24 hours).
- Borrows credibility (famous marketing expert).
- Appeals to budget-consciousness ($199).
- Is specific.
- Makes an offer.
- Is clear — doesn't ask the reader to figure out what it means.
- Gets the point across quickly.

The "uncreative" headline does seven distinct selling jobs in one sentence. A clever headline that produces a smile but no action does zero. There is no contest.

> **Bottom line: in advertising, it's not clever to be clever.**

**Don't write this — clever wordplay that sacrifices clarity:**

```text
Headline: Sole Mate.
[Subhead: Where shoes meet souls.]
Body: Step into a connection.
```

Why it fails: pun, abstract, says nothing about what's sold, who it's for, why they should care. Wins a design award. Sells nothing.

**Write this instead — clear, specific, sales-doing:**

```text
Headline: The dress shoe that fits a 10-hour shift without
killing your feet — designed by a podiatrist, $189.

Body: Most dress shoes are designed for 2-hour dinners, not
10-hour standing shifts. Dr. Helena Voss spent 14 years treating
nurses, hospitality staff, and trial lawyers with foot problems
caused by their shoes. So she designed her own…
```

Why it works:
- States exactly what's being sold.
- Names the pain (10-hour shift, foot pain).
- Borrows credibility (podiatrist, 14 years, named conditions).
- States the price up front.
- Identifies the target audience (workers on their feet).
- A reader skimming knows in 3 seconds whether this is for them.

### Where clever-creep typically appears

- **Headlines** — alliteration, puns, abstract teasers. The slot where clarity matters most.
- **Brand taglines** — vague aspirational phrases ("imagine more") that mean nothing.
- **Hero subheads** — "where X meets Y" constructions.
- **CTA buttons** — "let's go!" / "hop in!" instead of "Buy" / "Start free trial."
- **Section headers** — abstract titles that don't tell the reader what's in the section.

### The clever test

For every "clever" piece of copy, ask:

1. **Does it sell?** — does it do at least one of the seven jobs in the agency example (state, agitate, credibility, price, audience, specificity, offer)?
2. **Is it clearer than a plain version?** — write the boring version next to it. Compare.
3. **Would the audience get it in 1 second?** — clever that requires decoding kills attention.
4. **Does it survive being read alone?** — if it requires the visual / context to make sense, it's not strong enough as copy.

If "no" to any of these — cleverectomy. Cut it. Write the plain version.

### When clever IS okay

- When the clever version *also* does all the selling jobs (rare but real — see `headline-starters.md` for tested clever-but-also-clear patterns like #9 "Here's how a 95-pound granny made a 275-pound psychopathic killer cry like a baby").
- Brand campaigns whose ROI horizon is years, not weeks.
- Categories where in-group humor is part of the brand value (some hobbyist, some youth-targeted).
- When you have so much budget that brand fame is the goal.

### Common mistakes

- **Mistaking sophistication for cleverness** — sophisticated copy can still be plain. Plain isn't unsophisticated; it's confident.
- **"It's only clever to people in the industry"** — they're not your buyers. Stop writing for the awards committee.
- **Keeping a clever line because the team likes it** — internal love is the worst signal. Test against an audience.
- **Removing clever AND removing specificity** — replacing "clever" with "boring" is wrong. The right move is "clear AND specific AND sales-doing."

### When to apply

- Every headline.
- Every CTA.
- Every brand tagline.
- Every hero section.
- After a creative-heavy first draft, do a cleverectomy pass before publishing.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure brand-building campaigns where the brief explicitly is "memorability above conversion."
- High-trust audiences where clever is part of the in-group bond and the audience is small / loyal enough that clarity is assumed.
