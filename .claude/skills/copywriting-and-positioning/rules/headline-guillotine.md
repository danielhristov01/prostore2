## Use a human face — especially eyes — as the dominant visual to grab attention

**Impact: MEDIUM** — Human brains have a dedicated face-detection system (the fusiform face area). It fires faster than conscious recognition. A face in an ad is essentially a free attention-grab — the eye locks onto it whether the reader wants it to or not. The "Guillotine" name comes from how directly faces stop the eye, like a head dropping into the visual field.

Eye-direction matters too: a face *looking directly at the camera* maximally captures attention; a face *looking at the headline or product* directs the reader's eye toward what matters next. This is why so many high-converting landing pages have a hero image of a person looking either at the reader or at the CTA.

Note: this is technically a layout/visual rule, but its effect is so strong on whether the headline gets read that we file it under headline craft — the face is your headline's wingman.

**Don't write this — product-only image, no human element:**

```text
Hero section:
[Photo of a vacuum cleaner on a white background]
Headline: The new XR9 vacuum.
Body: Features and specs.
```

Why it fails: nothing for the face-detection system to engage. Reader's eye scans past. The headline has to do all the attention work alone.

**Write this instead — face leading, looking at the headline:**

```text
Hero section:
[Photo of a person mid-laugh, eyes directed slightly to the
right where the headline sits]

Headline: Three minutes a room. Pet hair gone.
Body: …
```

Why it works:
- Face-detection grabs the eye first.
- Face's gaze direction routes the reader's eye toward the headline.
- Emotion (laughter) sets the desired emotional tone before the words land.
- The product appears later, after engagement is established.

### Variations on the principle

- **Direct gaze (face looking at camera)** — maximum attention grab. Use for hero/above-fold.
- **Gaze direction toward CTA / headline** — directs reader's eye where you want it. Use for action-driving sections.
- **Multiple faces / crowd** — works as social-proof signal but dilutes the individual face effect. Use carefully.
- **Face of someone who looks like the prospect** — adds liking (`psych-weapons-of-influence.md`) on top of attention.
- **Face of an authority figure** — adds authority (`psych-transfer-credibility.md`) on top.

### Common mistakes

- **Stock-photo faces that look fake** — "diverse-team-laughing-at-laptop" stock photos read as fake to modern audiences. Use real customers/employees when possible.
- **Faces that look at nothing** — a face staring into space is wasted directional power. Aim the gaze.
- **Wrong demographic** — a face the audience doesn't identify with can repel attention as efficiently as the right face attracts it.
- **Face with no purpose** — adding a face just for face's sake adds clutter. The face should be doing a *job* (attention, identification, authority, emotion).
- **Tiny faces in busy layouts** — face-detection needs the face large enough to register. Hero-sized works; thumbnail-sized in a wall of clutter doesn't.

### When to apply

- Hero sections of landing pages.
- Display ads, social ads, print ads.
- Email banner imagery.
- Anywhere the page has to grab attention against scroll/scan velocity.

### When NOT to apply

- Highly technical B2B audiences making spec-sheet decisions — a face can read as fluff and undercut central-route credibility.
- Categories where the product itself is the star (food photography, fashion close-ups, hardware tools where the form factor is the pitch).
- When you don't have a real, on-brand face — fake face is worse than no face.
