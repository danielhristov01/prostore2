## Use product-in-use photos — they outpull product-only and text-only ads measurably

**Impact: HIGH** — Decades of split-test data converge: photos drive attention, in-use photos drive more attention than product-only shots, and photos with people drive more than photos without. Each layer compounds — they're additive, not exclusive.

The Starch Research findings:

- **Adding a product photo** vs. text-only: ~**13% lift** in attention.
- **Product-in-use photo** vs. product-only: another **~13% lift**.
- **Photos that include people** (vs. ads with no people / no visuals at all): **~25% lift** in "noted" scores.

Why in-use shots win: they add **action, drama, interest, and demonstration**. They engage the reader's imagination — the prospect mentally inserts themselves into the scene (`body-mental-movies.md`). Static product shots show what the thing IS; in-use shots show what it DOES, which is the benefit.

People in the shots compound the effect because of face-detection (`headline-guillotine.md`) and identification.

**Don't write this — text-only or product-only on a sterile background:**

```text
Headline: New X1 stand mixer.
[Photo: stand mixer on white background, side view]
```

Why it fails: shows what the product is, not what it does. Reader sees "stand mixer" and moves on. No imagination engaged.

**Write this instead — product in use, person visible, action implied:**

```text
Headline: The bread your kids ask for by name — in 90 minutes,
including rise.

[Photo: hands kneading dough in the X1, flour on the counter,
a child reaching up toward the bowl in soft kitchen light]

Body: …
```

Why it works:
- Product visible, in use.
- People (hands, child) — face/people effect.
- Action (kneading) — drama and movement.
- Scene (kitchen, flour, light) — sensory rendering.
- Reader inserts themselves into the scene; desire builds before the body copy is even read.

### The picture caption is undervalued

The eye stops at images. After looking at the image, the reader's eye usually moves to the caption *before* the body copy. Captions are heavily-read real estate — and most copy wastes it on filenames or generic descriptions.

**Don't write captions like this:**
> "Stand mixer, side view."

**Write captions like this:**
> "90 minutes from flour to fresh-baked bread — including the rise. The two-speed paddle and the heated bowl shave 30 minutes off most mixers."

Captions should sell, not describe.

### Image-quality matters

Stock photos with the obvious "stock photo look" (over-staged, fake-diverse-team-laughing-at-laptop) read as fake to modern audiences and undercut credibility. If you must use stock, choose images that don't scream stock. Original photography is worth the spend on hero placements.

### Common mistakes

- **No image at all** — biggest single picture mistake; leaves the attention multiplier on the table.
- **Product-only shot** when an in-use shot would work — leaves another 13% on the table.
- **Wasted captions** — describing the image instead of selling.
- **Bad stock photos** — undermines credibility worse than no photo at all.
- **Tiny images** in busy layouts — image needs presence to register.
- **Visual irrelevance** — a beautiful photo that doesn't tie to the offer is decoration, not persuasion.

### When to apply

- Every print, web, or social ad.
- Every product page hero.
- Every email banner.
- Sales pages — multiple in-use images throughout.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure text-only contexts (radio, podcast ads, plain-text email — though even there, descriptive language can render mental images).
- Categories where "no image" is a deliberate brand voice (some legal, financial, very minimalist tech).
