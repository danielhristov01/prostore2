## Use Power Visual Adjectives (PVAs) to render the product vividly in the prospect's mind

**Impact: HIGH** — Power Visual Adjectives are descriptive words that produce clear, bright, sensory mental images. Sweet, juicy, mouth-watering, crisp, golden, sun-drenched, hospital-bright, sparkling, gleaming, fluffy, deodorized, herb-seasoned. They make the prospect's brain *render* the product instead of merely reading about it.

The mechanism: when you read "Red Delicious apple," the brain pulls up a vague, generic placeholder. When you read "the sweetest, juiciest, most mouth-watering Red Delicious apple, hand-picked by my 73-year-old mother from sun-drenched orchards in our backyard," the brain renders a specific, sensory, emotional scene. That rendering is desire.

This is a sibling rule to `body-extreme-specificity.md` (numbers and counts) and `body-mental-movies.md` (full sensory scenes). PVAs are the *adjective-level* tool inside both.

A bonus: when one competitor uses PVAs and the others don't, the PVA-user looks more thorough, more confident, more authoritative — even when the underlying product is identical. The non-PVA competitors look generic by contrast. This is the asymmetric leverage of the technique.

**Don't write this — generic adjectives, vague verbs, no sensory grip:**

```text
Italian restaurant ad:

"People love our authentic Italian food because we prepare it
like we do for our own family. Try it, it's delicious!"
```

Why it fails: "authentic," "love," "delicious" are all words that evoke nothing specific. The brain pulls up a generic restaurant placeholder. Could describe any of 200,000 Italian restaurants in America.

**Write this instead — sensory, specific, picture-producing PVAs:**

```text
Italian restaurant ad:

"We make our pasta fresh every morning. We bake our own bread,
golden and crusty. Our sauce is made from scratch — never from
cans. Only fresh herbs go into our recipes. We use 100% virgin
olive oil, cold-pressed. Spring water fills your glass, mellow
Italian music fills the air, and softly glowing candles light
your table."
```

Why it works:
- **Sensory adjectives**: golden, crusty, fresh, mellow, softly glowing.
- **Specific verbs**: bake, make from scratch, fills.
- **Multiple senses engaged**: sight (golden bread, glowing candles), taste (fresh pasta, virgin olive oil), sound (music), feel (atmosphere).
- The brain renders a *specific* restaurant scene the prospect can step into.
- Competitors saying "great Italian food" can't compete with this rendered.

**Another example — cleaning service:**

```text
DON'T:
"Our expert cleaners will make your office sparkle like new."

DO:
"We make your walls and floors hospital-bright, your restrooms
sparkling and sanitized, your windows gleaming clean, and your
carpets fluffy, fresh, and deodorized."
```

The difference is the prospect sees their *own* office cleaned. The PVAs do the rendering.

### How to find the right PVAs

For each thing you sell, list the 5 senses and ask:

- **Sight** — what does it look like? (color, light, motion, scale)
- **Sound** — what does it sound like? (or what silence does it produce?)
- **Touch** — what does it feel like? (texture, weight, temperature)
- **Smell** — what does it smell like? (and what does it eliminate?)
- **Taste** — what does it taste like? (or what does the prospect taste while using it?)

Most copy uses sight only. Engaging touch, smell, and sound increases mental rendering substantially.

### Common mistakes

- **Generic adjectives** — "great," "wonderful," "amazing," "the best" produce no mental image. Banish from copy.
- **Adjective stacking without specificity** — "amazing, wonderful, delicious meals" is still generic, just longer. PVAs are sensory, not just descriptive.
- **Cliché PVAs** — "mouth-watering" and "luscious" worked in 1950 and still work, but for premium brands, find fresher language.
- **PVAs detached from the LF8 or USP** — the adjectives serve the benefit. Sensory description for its own sake is decoration.
- **Over-PVAing the small stuff** — every paragraph reading like food poetry exhausts the reader. Use PVAs at moments of impact (hero, key paragraphs), not constantly.

### When to apply

- Hero sections that need to render desire fast.
- Product description copy.
- Sensory categories (food, drink, hospitality, beauty, fragrance, fashion, real estate, travel, automotive).
- Any place a generic adjective is currently sitting.

### When NOT to apply

- Highly technical B2B audiences buying on spec — PVAs read as fluff there.
- Compliance-heavy categories (medical, financial, legal) where descriptive flourish raises liability concerns.
- Premium ultra-minimalist luxury where understatement is the brand voice.
