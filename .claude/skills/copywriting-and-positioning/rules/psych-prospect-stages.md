## Match the copy to the prospect's awareness stage (precontemplation → maintenance)

**Impact: HIGH** — A "buy now!" CTA shown to someone who's never heard of the product wastes the impression. A long product-introduction page shown to a returning customer wastes the visit. Different stages need different copy. Most ad failures are stage mismatches, not message failures.

The five stages a prospect moves through:

1. **Precontemplation** — doesn't know your product exists, or doesn't know they need it. Goal: introduce category and pain.
2. **Contemplation** — aware the product exists, has thought about using it. Goal: build interest and stakes.
3. **Preparation** — ready to consider buying, but needs more info (price, comparison, fit). Goal: differentiate and reduce risk.
4. **Action** — buying. Goal: remove friction and close.
5. **Maintenance** — already a customer; product is part of life. Goal: retention, upsell, advocacy.

Two strategic options once you know stages exist:

- **Cover all stages in one piece** — long-form sales pages do this; reader self-selects the section relevant to their stage.
- **Series across stages** — ads, emails, or pages that progress with the prospect over time.

**Don't write this — pure action-stage copy shown cold to precontemplation prospects:**

```text
Cold ad to first-time visitors:

Headline: Buy the X1 Treadmill — 30% off this week only!

Body: Order now. Free shipping. 30-day return.
[Buy Now button]
```

Why it fails: visitor has no idea what makes this treadmill worth buying, or whether they even need a treadmill. The CTA arrives before interest exists. Conversion will be terrible.

**Write this instead — staged journey, each piece sized to the stage:**

```text
Cold ad (Precontemplation):
"Most dads over 40 are six minutes slower than their kids when
they hit a hill. Here's what that does to your knees by 50."

→ Click leads to article about fitness decline + intro of treadmill category as a solution.

Retargeting ad (Contemplation):
"You read about the dad-fitness gap last week. Here's the home
gym setup that fixed it for 4,200 of them."

→ Click leads to product page with comparison + testimonials.

Pricing-page ad (Preparation):
"Compare the X1 to commercial-grade treadmills. Same 4HP motor,
$1,800 less."

→ Click leads to checkout-adjacent page with risk reversal.

Cart ad (Action):
"Free white-glove delivery and 90-day return — even if you change
your mind. Lock in this price before Friday."
```

Why it works: each ad meets the prospect at the stage they're in. None of them try to do the whole job alone.

### Common mistakes

- **Treating all traffic the same** — cold paid traffic, retargeting, and email subscribers are at different stages. Same copy across all is malpractice.
- **Skipping precontemplation** in mature markets — "everybody knows about treadmills" is true, but "everybody knows about *your* treadmill brand" usually isn't.
- **Forgetting maintenance** — most copy budget goes to acquisition, but stage-5 retention/upsell is where margin lives.
- **Demanding action before interest** — the #1 cause of low landing-page conversion.

### When to apply

- Always when planning a campaign or page set.
- When response is poor — diagnose by checking if copy and stage are aligned before rewriting copy.
- Designing email sequences and ad funnels.

### When NOT to apply

- Single-ad campaigns where you have to do the whole job in one impression — fall back to long-form, all-stages-in-one (see Vercel-style "kitchen sink" sales pages, Schwartz-style long copy).
