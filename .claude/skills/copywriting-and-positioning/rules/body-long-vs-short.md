## Long copy outsells short copy when the prospect is genuinely interested — write enough to fully sell

**Impact: HIGH** — *"The most common expression you hear about advertising is that people will not read much. Yet a vast amount of the best-paying advertising shows that people do read much."* — Claude Hopkins.

The myth: "people don't read." The truth: people don't read *uninteresting* copy. A real prospect for the product — someone with the problem your product solves and the means to buy — will read every word that helps them justify the decision.

The salesperson analogy: a buyer would gladly spend two hours with a great salesperson rather than ten minutes with a mediocre one. The two-hour pitch hits every benefit, addresses every objection, builds desire from multiple angles. Why would a written sales letter be different? It's the same job, just on paper.

The principle:

> "The only reason for using short copy is when there isn't much to say." — Maxwell Sackheim

Write *as long as the product needs* to be fully sold. No longer (don't pad), no shorter (don't undersell).

**The mechanism for why long copy wins:**

- More words = more chances to hit each prospect's specific hot button.
- More benefits, more justifications, more proof — all stack on length-implies-strength (`psych-heuristics.md`).
- Multiple awareness-stage prospects served on the same page (`psych-prospect-stages.md`).
- Objections handled in advance instead of left to bounce the reader.
- Mental movies (`body-mental-movies.md`) need room to render.

**Don't write this — abbreviated copy that leaves prospects hanging:**

```text
Hero:
"Better treadmills. Buy now. $1,299."
[Buy button]
```

Why it fails: a $1,299 purchase decision can't be made on six words. The committed prospect has 20 questions; this answers zero of them. Conversion is whatever fraction of prospects were already going to buy regardless.

**Write this instead — long copy that does the entire sales job:**

```text
[Hero with biggest-benefit headline]

[Mental movie of the LF8 outcome — `body-mental-movies.md`]

[The problem with most home treadmills — agitating the pain]

[How this one is built differently — feature-to-benefit translation]

[Specifics — numbers, materials, engineers, testing protocols]

[6 testimonials with photos, names, cities]

[Comparison chart vs. competitors]

[Common objections handled — "Won't this break in 2 years?" "Is the
4HP motor enough?" "What if I can't fit it in my room?" — each
answered]

[Long warranty / guarantee — `offer-guarantees.md`]

[Pricing + financing options]

[14 more testimonials]

[Scarcity / deadline — `offer-scarcity.md`]

[Strong, specific CTA]

[Logos of "as featured in," partner brands, certifications]

[FAQ — 20 questions answered]

[Final CTA with risk reversal]
```

Why it works:
- Every prospect at every stage finds what they need.
- Every objection is handled before it can stall the decision.
- Length itself signals strength to skim-readers.
- Engaged readers go deep; uninterested readers leave (and would have left a short page too).

### The misunderstood A/B test

Long-copy-loses-to-short-copy in tests almost always means: "this particular long copy was bad, and short copy was a more honest version of nothing-to-say." Long copy that's lazy, repetitive, or padded loses. Long copy that's tight, varied, and serves real prospects wins. Don't conclude length is the variable; tightness is.

### Common mistakes

- **Believing the "people don't read" myth** without testing it on your audience.
- **Padding for length** — fluff is worse than brevity. Every paragraph must do a job.
- **One-page-fits-all** — sometimes the right answer is a short ad linking to a long sales page. Match length to context.
- **Long copy without skimmability** — large blocks with no subheads, no bullets, no testimonials breaking up the flow. Long copy needs scannable structure.
- **Same density throughout** — opening must hook fast; middle can elaborate; close must accelerate.

### When to apply (default to longer)

- High-ticket purchases.
- Complex products with multiple benefits and objections.
- Products with significant prospect skepticism or unfamiliarity.
- Sales pages, sales letters, B2B landing pages.
- Email sequences (cumulative length across messages).
- Categories where the prospect needs to justify the spend (to themselves, partner, boss).

### When to apply (short copy)

- Low-ticket impulse purchases.
- Very simple value propositions ("$1 hot dog Tuesday").
- Branded-search landing pages where intent is already high.
- Display/social ads designed to drive traffic to a longer page.
- Brand-awareness placements where length isn't the job.

### When the right answer is "long copy hidden in a short page"

Modern web pattern: short hero + expandable sections. Skim readers see enough to commit; engaged readers expand to see everything. Best of both worlds.
