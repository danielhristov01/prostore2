## Open with a question to install an "open loop" the brain has to close

**Impact: MEDIUM** — A question creates an *open loop* in the reader's brain — an incomplete pattern that the brain wants to resolve. Once a question is asked, the brain quietly searches for the answer until it finds one. That search is what keeps the reader reading.

The mechanism: human cognition is pattern-completing. Unfinished patterns generate mild tension; resolution releases it. A question is a crisp open pattern. Statement headlines don't have this property — the brain doesn't *need* the rest of the copy to "finish" anything.

This rule overlaps with starters #18-21 in `headline-starters.md` (DO YOU / WOULD YOU / CAN YOU / IF YOU). It also applies to body-copy openers, sales-letter openers, and section subheads.

**Don't write this — flat statement that demands no response:**

```text
Headline: We have great coupon offers.
```

Why it fails: no open loop. The reader's brain has nothing to complete. They scroll past.

**Write this instead — open-loop question matched to the audience's pain:**

```text
Headline: What if the next coupon you used was worth $200 instead
of $2?
```

Why it works:
- Open loop installed → brain wants to know how that's possible.
- Concrete numbers ($200 vs $2) frame the gap and make the question feel answerable.
- The reader keeps reading specifically to close the loop.

Or:

```text
Headline: Do you know which coupon trick the top 3% of bargain
shoppers use that the rest don't?
```

Why it works:
- Open loop + identification ("top 3%" / "the rest").
- Triggers liking/aspiration (LF8 want #6 — be superior).
- Reader either thinks "no, I don't" or "I think I do" — either answer keeps them reading to verify.

### Where else open-loop questions work

- **First sentence of the body copy** (after the headline). Cashvertising recommends starting sales letters with a question.
- **Section subheads** in long copy — re-engages skimmers at each section.
- **Email subject lines** — outperform statement subject lines in many tests.
- **Hero sub-copy under a statement headline** — combination pattern: bold statement + question that makes the reader want the explanation.

### Common mistakes

- **Yes/no questions the reader can dismiss in one syllable** — "Want to save money?" / "No." / scroll. Frame the question so a one-word answer doesn't kill the loop.
- **Rhetorical questions whose answer is obvious and uninteresting** — "Don't you want a great life?" The brain answers "yes, obviously" and moves on without engagement.
- **Question without follow-through** — opening a loop the body copy doesn't close. Reader feels manipulated, bounces.
- **Stacking too many questions** — one strong question per section. A wall of questions reads as interrogation.
- **Question-form clickbait that doesn't deliver** — "Will THIS one trick double your income?" → 4,000 words about something else. Trust burns fast.

### Caveat — not all research agrees

Some studies suggest rhetorical questions (the kind where the answer is obvious) can have *no significant influence* on persuasion, or even cause readers to feel pressured and read more critically. The strongest takeaway: **questions help retention more than they help persuasion**. They keep the reader engaged, but they don't replace strong arguments.

### When to apply

- When a statement headline feels flat and you want to inject curiosity.
- Opening sentence of any sales letter or long-form piece.
- Section subheads in long-form copy.
- Email subject lines that need to compete in a busy inbox.

### When NOT to apply

- When the answer is obvious and uninteresting (no loop tension).
- High-stakes B2B where executive-summary statements work better than questions.
- When you can't credibly close the loop in the body — broken promises burn trust faster than they generate clicks.
