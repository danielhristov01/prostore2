## Use two-sided messaging — acknowledge competitors before differentiating

**Impact: MEDIUM** — One-sided messaging ("we're great") triggers ad-defense — the brain treats it as biased. Two-sided messaging ("they're good at X, we're better at Y") feels balanced, which the prospect's brain reads as more trustworthy. The bonus: once the prospect has accepted you as fair, they're more open to your differentiation claims AND start questioning the one-sided pitches from your competitors.

The Cashvertising example:

> *"Acme makes an excellent fly swatter, and they've been doing so for years. Heck, in the 1940s and '50s they were the most popular way to kill the pesky critters. And they did a darn good job back then. However, now it's the 21st century. And it's time to step up to full-scale fly-swatting automation, with our new RoboSwat Laser-Powered Anti Fly Gun Turret. So easy and effective, it makes old-fashioned (and messy) swatters obsolete!"*

Notice: Acme is *complimented* (excellent, popular, did a darn good job). Then the world is reframed (it's the 21st century now). Then the new product takes the lead. The reader feels the writer was fair to Acme — which makes them trust the comparison that follows.

### How two-sidedness works mechanically

- **Peripheral-route reader** sees a "thoughtful, even-handed" tone and assumes the message is more credible than competitors' aggressive one-sided claims.
- **Central-route reader** processes the concession + counter and ends up *more* skeptical of the competitor's claims (since this writer just modeled what fair argument looks like).

Either way, you win.

### The comparison chart variant

Comparison charts that point out *your* advantages (while acknowledging the competitor's general legitimacy) are extremely effective. The reader's lazy brain reads the chart and concludes "all the research has been done for me, I'll take this one." Central-route readers will check, but most readers won't.

### What the concession should NOT be

The concession must be **honest but not damaging.** Concede where the competitor is genuinely good — but in dimensions that don't matter as much as the dimension where YOU win.

| Strong concession (safe) | Dangerous concession (don't) |
|---|---|
| "They have more colors" | "They have a better warranty" (warranty is decision-critical) |
| "They've been around longer" | "They have lower prices" (price is decision-critical) |
| "Their interface is well-designed" | "Their support team is faster" (support is decision-critical) |

The trick: concede things that *sound* generous but don't undermine the buying decision. Then own the dimensions that do.

**Don't write this — pure one-sided pitch:**

```text
Body: We're the best CRM on the market. Easy to use. Powerful
features. Top-rated support. Try us today!
```

Why it fails: every competitor says this. Reader's ad-defense fires. No reason to believe.

**Write this instead — two-sided, concession + differentiation:**

```text
Body: HubSpot built CRM as we know it. They invented the inbound
methodology, their academy is genuinely good, and if you have a
team of 50+ marketers, their suite makes sense.

But we noticed something: 73% of our customers come from teams
of 5-15 people who tried HubSpot and found it overwhelming for
their stage. So we built [Product] specifically for that team
size — fewer features, dramatically faster setup, 1/4 the
monthly cost.

If you're under 15 people and want to ship in a week instead of
plan for a quarter, this is the comparison:

[Comparison chart]
```

Why it works:
- Genuine compliment to HubSpot — establishes fairness.
- Concession (HubSpot is right for big teams) is honest but doesn't hurt the pitch.
- Differentiation is sharp on the dimension that matters for THIS audience.
- Reader trusts the writer because they didn't pretend HubSpot is bad.

### Common mistakes

- **Damaging concessions** — admitting weakness on a decision-critical dimension. Never.
- **Token compliments** — "they're a fine company, but…" reads as fake fairness. Compliments must be specific and earned.
- **Bashing the competitor in the differentiation phase** — concession should set up *strength contrast*, not personal attack. Stay civil; the contrast does the work.
- **Forgetting which audience this works on** — works best on educated, skeptical, central-route audiences. Less effective on peripheral-route impulse buyers (where simple one-sided assertion sometimes outpulls).
- **Concession without differentiation** — being fair without then making the case for yourself just helps the competitor.

### When to apply

- Direct competitive head-to-heads where the prospect is comparing options.
- Markets with sophisticated buyers (B2B, considered consumer, professional categories).
- Positioning copy that needs to define *who you are vs. who you're not*.
- Categories where bashing the competitor would feel low-class.

### When NOT to apply

- Highly emotional / impulse categories where one-sided assertion is the convention.
- When you have nothing genuinely good to concede (just don't mention competitors at all).
- Pre-launch / awareness stage where competitor mentions would educate the prospect about competition they didn't know existed.
