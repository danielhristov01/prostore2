## Direct mental movies in the prospect's mind by engaging all five senses (V-A-K-O-G)

**Impact: CRITICAL** — The brain doesn't store memories or anticipations as text — it stores them as multi-sensory internal representations (IRs). When you describe an experience using all five senses, the prospect's brain *renders* the experience as if it's happening — and they feel its value before they've paid for it. That pre-felt value is what moves them to act.

**V-A-K-O-G — the five sensory channels:**

| Channel | What it covers |
|---|---|
| **V**isual | What you see — color, light, motion, scale |
| **A**uditory | What you hear — sounds, silence, voices, music |
| **K**inesthetic | What you feel physically and emotionally — texture, temperature, weight, mood |
| **O**lfactory | What you smell |
| **G**ustatory | What you taste |

Most copy uses Visual only. Engaging two more channels — usually Kinesthetic and one of A/O/G — multiplies the rendering impact. The fully-rendered scene is what stays with the reader after they close the page.

This is the elder cousin of `body-power-visual-adjectives.md` (sentence-level sensory adjectives) and `proof-examples-vs-statistics.md` (the example side of evidence). Mental movies are scene-level — full vignettes the reader plays in their head.

**Don't write this — abstract bug-killing pitch:**

```text
"Would you be more repulsed by having to kill a few bugs?"
```

Why it fails: the reader's brain doesn't render. "Bugs" is abstract. There's nothing to feel.

**Write this instead — full-sensory mental movie:**

```text
"Destroy an active nest of angry black widow spiders, including
a quivering web of their just-hatched-hot eggs, guarded by their
mindless, poison-fanged mother?

Don't forget that just one female black widow can blow out four
to nine egg sacs every summer, each containing up to 750 eggs,
incubating a fresh new load of up to 750 spiderlings in just 14
to 30 days.

Worse still, each venomous arachnid lives — no, mates and
breeds! — inside your home with your children and pets, for up
to three full years.

Try killing them yourself if you must, but think about this: if
you miss killing just one egg-engorged female (or one of her
hundreds of nearly microscopic crawling babies), you'll suffer
a full-blown arachnid infestation in no time.

I've been at war with black widows for the past five years. And
there's nothing worse than feeling something crawling across
your skin at night when you're in bed.

Well, actually, one thing is worse… discovering that the queen
mother blew out a pile of fresh hot eggs under your pillow."
```

Why it works:
- **V** — quivering web, microscopic crawling babies, egg sacs.
- **A** — implied (whispering crawl).
- **K** — feeling something crawling across your skin at night, the visceral revulsion of fresh eggs under the pillow.
- **K (emotional)** — fear, anger, dread, urgency.
- The brain renders the entire scene. The reader is now in the bedroom, feeling it. They will pay almost any price to make it stop.

### Positive-direction example

The same technique can render a *desired* future, not just a feared one. Cruise example from `proof-examples-vs-statistics.md`:

```text
"7am. You're on the private balcony of your suite, robe wrapped
around you, the smell of fresh espresso your butler just
delivered, watching the morning sun light up the cliffs of
Santorini as the ship glides into port."

V — sun on cliffs, balcony view.
K — robe, morning warmth, glide.
O — fresh espresso.
A — implied (calm morning, distant ship sounds).
```

Reader pre-feels the vacation. The decision to book is just buying back into the scene already playing in their head.

### How to write a mental movie

1. **Pick the scene** — the moment of maximum LF8 satisfaction (or LF8 threat, for fear-based copy).
2. **Set the time and place** — concrete: "7am, Tuesday, your kitchen…"
3. **Engage at least three senses** — V is automatic; deliberately add K + one of A/O/G.
4. **Use specific detail** (`body-extreme-specificity.md`) — "espresso" beats "coffee"; "Santorini" beats "Mediterranean."
5. **Put the reader in the scene** with second-person ("you") and present-tense.
6. **Pace** — short sentences for tension, longer ones for atmosphere.

### Common mistakes

- **Visual-only scenes** — describing what something *looks* like but not what it feels like, smells like, sounds like.
- **Generic scenes** — "imagine a perfect vacation" doesn't render. Specific scenes do.
- **Past tense** — pulls the reader out. Present tense puts them in.
- **Third person** — "the customer enjoys…" reads as a brochure. "You feel…" reads as experience.
- **Mental-movie scenes that don't tie to the LF8** — pretty for pretty's sake. Every scene should be the LF8 want satisfied (or threatened).
- **Mental movies in transactional moments** — the cart page is not the place for sensory poetry. Movies belong in the desire-building stage.

### When to apply

- Hero copy — render the LF8 outcome the product delivers.
- Body copy at the desire stage of AIDA.
- Fear-based copy (`psych-fear.md`) — render the threat to make it real.
- Aspirational/lifestyle copy — render the post-purchase reality.
- Any product where "imagine using this…" is the persuasion job.

### When NOT to apply

- Action-stage copy (cart, checkout, pricing tables) — the movie was supposed to play earlier.
- Spec-sheet B2B where central-route audit happens — render at the case-study level, not in the spec table.
- Brand voice that's deliberately understated — reduce intensity, don't abandon technique.
