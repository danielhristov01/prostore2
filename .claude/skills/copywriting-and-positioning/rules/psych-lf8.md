## Tie every appeal to one of the eight Life-Force wants (LF8)

**Impact: CRITICAL** — This is the foundation rule. Every other psych rule (`psych-fear`, `psych-ego`, `psych-social-beings`…) is a tactic for activating one or more of these eight wants. If your copy doesn't ladder up to at least one LF8, it's selling on a weaker, learned want and leaving response on the table.

The eight Life-Force wants — biologically programmed, present in every human, no learning required:

1. **Survival** — enjoyment of life, life extension.
2. **Enjoyment of food and drink.**
3. **Freedom from fear, pain, and danger.**
4. **Sexual companionship.**
5. **Comfortable living conditions.**
6. **To be superior** — winning, keeping up with the Joneses.
7. **Care and protection of loved ones.**
8. **Social approval.**

Below the LF8 sit nine **secondary, learned wants**: to be informed, curiosity, cleanliness, efficiency, convenience, dependability/quality, expression of beauty/style, economy/profit, bargains. These work — but always weaker than the LF8.

The most effective copy causes the prospect to **visualize themselves experiencing** the satisfaction of an LF8 want via your product, builds the desire, and then makes them believe your product actually delivers it (credibility — see `proof-evidence.md`, `proof-social-proof.md`) and pushes them to act (`cta-battling-inertia.md`).

**Don't write this — selling on a secondary, learned want when an LF8 is available:**

```text
Headline: Our home security system is dependable and affordable.

Body: Reliable 24/7 monitoring at a great price. Easy installation.
Industry-leading customer service.
```

Why it fails: anchored to "dependability" (secondary want) and "economy" (secondary want). The product's actual lever — **want #7 (care and protection of loved ones)** and **want #3 (freedom from fear, pain, danger)** — never appears. Generic, swappable, weak.

**Write this instead — leading with the LF8 the product actually serves:**

```text
Headline: When your kids are home alone, will the locks be enough?

Body: Most break-ins happen between 10 a.m. and 3 p.m. — the
hours your house is empty and your kids are due home from school.
A dead-bolt isn't going to stop a man with a crowbar. Our 24/7
monitored system calls the police within 30 seconds of an
intrusion — so your children are protected even when you're not
there.
```

Why it works:
- **Want #7 — care and protection of loved ones** — the entire frame is about kids being safe.
- **Want #3 — freedom from fear** — the threat is named and a remedy offered.
- The "dependability" and "affordability" facts can come later as supporting evidence; they were never the lever.

### Common mistakes

- **Defaulting to want #6 (be superior) for every product** — works for luxury, fails for utility.
- **Stacking too many LF8 in one ad** — pick the dominant one. Splash damage from secondaries is fine; competing primaries dilute.
- **Mistaking a feature for a want** — features satisfy wants; wants are emotional. "500GB storage" is a feature; "never lose a photo of your kids again" is want #7.
- **Selling secondary wants when an LF8 is available** — the LF8 always outpulls. Always. If a competitor anchors on the LF8 and you anchor on "convenience," they win.

### When to apply

- Always. Every persuasive piece of copy should ladder up to at least one LF8. This is the rule that the others depend on.

### When NOT to apply

- Pure information copy where no purchase is being asked (technical docs, internal memos). Otherwise: always.
